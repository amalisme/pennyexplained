What is a Bank Fee?

A bank fee is money your bank charges you for certain services or account activities. These fees can range from a few dollars to $35 or more, depending on what triggered the charge.

Banks charge fees to cover their costs of providing services and, in some cases, to make profit. Understanding bank fees helps you avoid unnecessary charges and keep more of your money.

Why Banks Charge Fees

Banks are businesses that need to cover their operating costs. They pay for buildings, ATMs, employees, computer systems, and security measures. Some of this cost is covered by interest they earn from loans, but they also charge fees for specific services.

Some fees encourage certain behaviors. For example, overdraft fees discourage you from spending more than you have. Monthly maintenance fees sometimes encourage you to maintain a minimum balance or use direct deposit.

Common Types of Bank Fees

Monthly maintenance fees are recurring charges just for having the account. These typically range from $5 to $15 per month. Many banks waive this fee if you meet certain conditions, like keeping a minimum balance of $500 or having direct deposit set up.

Overdraft fees occur when you spend more money than you have in your account. If you have $50 but try to spend $70, the bank might cover the $20 difference but charge you a feeâ€”often $35 per transaction. These can add up quickly if you make multiple transactions while overdrawn.

ATM fees happen when you use an ATM that doesn't belong to your bank's network. Your bank might charge $2 to $3, and the ATM owner might also charge $2 to $3, totaling $4 to $6 per withdrawal.

How Much Bank Fees Cost

Let's look at real numbers to see how fees add up. If you pay a $12 monthly maintenance fee, that's $144 per year. Add three overdraft fees at $35 each ($105), plus using out-of-network ATMs 10 times at $5 each ($50), and you've spent $299 in fees for the year.

That $299 could have stayed in your account if you'd avoided these fees. It's enough to cover groceries for a month, a small emergency expense, or a significant portion of a bill.

When Banks Charge Fees

Some fees are charged on a schedule. Monthly maintenance fees are deducted automatically, usually on the same day each month (like the 1st or the 15th). The bank takes the fee directly from your account balance.

Other fees are triggered by specific actions. If you overdraw your account on Tuesday, the overdraft fee appears in your account on Tuesday or Wednesday. If you use an out-of-network ATM, both fees (your bank's and the ATM owner's) appear within a day or two.

Fees Show Up in Your Account

When a bank charges a fee, it appears as a transaction in your account, just like a purchase or withdrawal. You'll see it listed in your transaction history with a description like "Monthly Maintenance Fee" or "Overdraft Fee."

Here's what your transaction history might look like:

  • March 1: Monthly Maintenance Fee: -$12
  • March 5: Overdraft Fee: -$35
  • March 10: ATM Fee (Out of Network): -$3

These fees reduce your account balance just like spending money. If you started with $200 and were charged these three fees, your new balance would be $150.

Understanding Fee Disclosures

Banks must tell you about their fees before you open an account. This information is usually in a document called a "fee schedule" or "account agreement."

This document lists every possible fee: what triggers it, how much it costs, and whether there are ways to avoid it. For example, it might say "Monthly maintenance fee: $10. Waived with $500 minimum daily balance or direct deposit."

You can usually find your bank's current fee schedule on their website. It's worth reviewing once a year to know what fees you might encounter.

Difference Between Fees and Interest

Bank fees and interest are both money moving in or out of your account, but they work differently. Fees are charges you pay to the bank for services or account activities. Interest is money the bank pays you (in savings accounts) or that you pay to the bank (on loans).

For example, you might pay a $10 monthly fee for checking but earn $8 monthly interest on your savings. The fee is a service charge; the interest is earnings on your deposited money.

How Fees Affect Your Balance

Every fee charged reduces your account balance immediately. This can sometimes create a chain reaction of fees if you're not careful.

Here's an example: You have $40 in your account. Your $12 monthly fee is charged (new balance: $28). Then an automatic bill for $35 tries to process. You don't have enough money, so you get hit with a $35 overdraft fee. Now your balance is -$42, and you might face additional daily fees until you deposit money.

Fees at Different Types of Banks

Traditional banks with physical branches tend to charge more fees. They have higher operating costs (buildings, staff, etc.) and often charge monthly maintenance fees of $10 to $15.

Online banks typically charge fewer or lower fees because they have lower operating costs. Many online banks offer no monthly maintenance fees, no minimum balance requirements, and even reimburse ATM fees you incur at other banks' ATMs.

Credit unions are member-owned institutions that often charge lower fees than traditional banks. Their goal isn't profit, so they typically have lower or fewer fees overall.

Notification of Fees

Most banks notify you when a fee is charged. You might receive an email, a text message, or an app notification saying something like "A $35 overdraft fee was charged to your account ending in 1234."

Your monthly statement will also list all fees charged during that period. This helps you see exactly what you paid and why.

If you don't understand why you were charged a fee, you can call your bank's customer service. They can explain the specific transaction or account activity that triggered the fee.

Can Fees Be Reversed?

Sometimes banks will reverse fees, especially if it's your first time being charged or if there were extenuating circumstances. For example, if you've never had an overdraft fee before and you call to explain what happened, the bank might refund it as a courtesy.

However, this is at the bank's discretion. They're not required to refund fees, and they may only do it once or twice. Repeated requests for fee refunds are unlikely to be granted.

Key Takeaways

  • Bank fees are charges for account services or certain activities
  • Common fees include monthly maintenance, overdraft, and ATM fees
  • Fees reduce your account balance just like spending money
  • Banks must disclose all fees in writing before you open an account
  • Many fees can be avoided by meeting certain requirements
  • Online banks and credit unions typically charge lower fees than traditional banks
