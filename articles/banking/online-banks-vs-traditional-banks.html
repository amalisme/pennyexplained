Online Banks vs Traditional Banks

Banking 7 min read

Online banks and traditional brick-and-mortar banks each have advantages. Understanding the differences helps you choose what works best.

Advertisement - Leaderboard (728x90)

When opening a bank account, you'll choose between online banks and traditional banks. Online banks operate entirely through websites and apps with no physical branches. Traditional banks have physical locations you can walk into for in-person service.

Both types offer similar basic services like checking and savings accounts. The main differences are in fees, interest rates, and how you interact with the bank.

What Are Online Banks?

Online banks (also called digital banks or internet banks) have no physical branches. You do all your banking through a mobile app or website. Examples include Ally Bank, Marcus by Goldman Sachs, and Discover Bank.

These banks save money by not paying for buildings, tellers, or large staff. They pass these savings to customers through higher interest rates and lower fees.

What Are Traditional Banks?

Traditional banks (brick-and-mortar banks) have physical branches where you can meet with bank employees in person. Examples include Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, and local community banks.

These banks offer the full range of banking services in person, but operating physical locations costs money. This often results in lower interest rates and higher fees compared to online banks.

Online Banks: Pros and Cons

Advantages of Online Banks

  • Higher interest rates: Savings accounts often earn 3-5% APY compared to 0.01-0.5% at traditional banks
  • Lower fees: Many have no monthly maintenance fees, no minimum balance requirements, and free ATM reimbursements
  • 24/7 access: Bank anytime through apps, no need to visit during business hours
  • Better mobile apps: Often more user-friendly since the app is their main interface
  • Easy transfers: Quick electronic transfers between accounts

Example: You deposit $5,000 in an online savings account earning 4.5% APY. After one year, you earn $225 in interest. The same amount at a traditional bank earning 0.5% would only earn $25.

Disadvantages of Online Banks

  • No cash deposits: Can't walk in and deposit cash directly (though some partner with retailers)
  • No in-person help: Customer service is phone or chat only
  • Check deposits take longer: Must use mobile check deposit, which can take 1-3 days
  • May need another bank: If you regularly handle cash, you might need a traditional bank too
  • Learning curve: Some people prefer in-person banking, especially older adults

Traditional Banks: Pros and Cons

Advantages of Traditional Banks

  • Physical branches: Visit in person for help, advice, or complex transactions
  • Cash deposits: Easily deposit cash at any branch or ATM
  • Face-to-face service: Some people prefer talking to a real person
  • Full banking services: Mortgages, car loans, safe deposit boxes all in one place
  • Immediate help: Walk in and get problems resolved on the spot
  • Local presence: Community banks often support local businesses and causes

Example: You run a small cash business and deposit $500-1,000 in cash weekly. A traditional bank with nearby branches makes this simple - just stop by on your way home.

Disadvantages of Traditional Banks

  • Lower interest rates: Savings accounts often earn less than 0.5% APY
  • Higher fees: Monthly maintenance fees ($5-15), overdraft fees ($25-35), and various service charges
  • Minimum balances: Many require $500-1,500 minimum to avoid monthly fees
  • Limited hours: Branches close evenings and weekends
  • Inconvenient locations: May need to drive to find a branch

Example: Your traditional bank charges $12 monthly maintenance fee unless you keep a $1,500 minimum balance. Over a year, that's $144 in fees if you don't meet the requirement.

Which Type Should You Choose?

The best choice depends on your personal banking habits and needs. Here's how to decide:

Choose an Online Bank If:

  • You rarely deposit cash
  • You want to maximize interest on savings
  • You're comfortable with apps and online banking
  • You want to avoid monthly fees
  • You're building an emergency fund and want higher returns

Choose a Traditional Bank If:

  • You frequently deposit cash
  • You prefer in-person customer service
  • You want all banking services in one place
  • You're not comfortable with online-only banking
  • You value local branch access

Use Both

Many people use both types of banks. For example, keep your main checking account at a traditional bank for easy cash deposits and bill payments. Keep your savings at an online bank to earn higher interest.

Example: You keep $2,000 in a traditional bank checking account for bills and ATM access. You keep your $10,000 emergency fund in an online savings account earning 4.5% APY ($450 per year in interest).

Safety and Security

Both online and traditional banks are equally safe if they're FDIC insured. FDIC insurance protects up to $250,000 per depositor, per bank, regardless of whether it's online or has physical branches.

Always verify a bank is FDIC insured before opening an account. Look for the FDIC logo on their website or check the FDIC's BankFind tool online.

Making the Switch

If you're considering switching from traditional to online banking (or vice versa), take it slowly:

  1. Open the new account but keep your old one
  2. Transfer a small amount to test the new account
  3. Gradually move automatic payments and direct deposits
  4. Once everything works smoothly, close the old account

Never close your old account until you're sure the new one meets all your needs. This prevents problems with missed payments or lost access to funds.

The Future of Banking

Traditional banks are adding better mobile apps and online features. Online banks are partnering with retailers and ATM networks to offer more cash access. The line between the two is blurring.

Some traditional banks now match online banks' interest rates for new customers. Some online banks offer video chat with bankers for a more personal touch. The best choice for you may change over time as both types evolve.

Key Takeaways

  • Online banks offer higher interest rates (3-5%) and lower fees but no physical branches
  • Traditional banks provide in-person service and easy cash deposits but lower interest rates (0.01-0.5%)
  • Both types are equally safe if FDIC insured up to $250,000
  • Many people use both - checking at traditional banks, savings at online banks
  • Your best choice depends on how often you deposit cash and your comfort with technology
  • The gap between online and traditional banks is shrinking as both add new features
