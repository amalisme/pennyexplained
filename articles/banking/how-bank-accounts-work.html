How Bank Accounts Work

Banking 7 min read

Learn the mechanics of bank accounts - how deposits, withdrawals, and transactions work behind the scenes.

Advertisement - Leaderboard (728x90)

Bank accounts work through a system of deposits, withdrawals, and electronic record-keeping. The bank doesn't literally store your cash in a vault with your name on it. Instead, they track your balance electronically and use your money to make loans to others.

The Deposit Process

When you deposit money into your bank account, here's what happens:

  • You provide money: Cash, check, or electronic transfer
  • Bank records it: They add the amount to your account balance
  • Money goes into reserves: Your physical cash joins the bank's reserves
  • You can access it: The balance is available for withdrawal or spending

Example: You deposit a $1,000 paycheck. The bank credits your account $1,000. The check gets processed through the banking system. Within 1-2 business days, the funds are fully available.

The Withdrawal Process

When you withdraw money:

  • You request money: At ATM, teller, or through debit card purchase
  • Bank checks balance: Ensures you have enough money
  • Bank debits account: Subtracts amount from your balance
  • You receive money: Cash from ATM, or merchant gets paid
Advertisement - Rectangle (300x250)

Your Balance

Your account balance is the current amount of money in your account. It changes with every transaction:

  • Available balance: Money you can use right now
  • Current balance: Includes pending transactions
  • Pending transactions: Purchases or deposits still processing

How Banks Use Your Money

Banks don't keep all deposited money in a vault. They use it to:

  • Make loans to other customers (they charge interest)
  • Keep required reserves (typically 10% of deposits)
  • Earn profit from the difference between loan interest and savings interest

This is why banks can afford to provide free checking accounts and pay interest on savings - they make money by lending out your deposits.

Key Takeaways

  • Deposits add money to your balance; withdrawals subtract
  • Banks track your money electronically, not in physical storage
  • Your balance shows how much money you can access
  • Banks use deposits to make loans and earn profit
  • FDIC insurance protects your deposits up to $250,000
