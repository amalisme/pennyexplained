What is a Bank Fee?

Banking 5 min read

Bank fees are charges that banks impose for various services and account activities. Understanding these fees helps you avoid unnecessary costs.

Advertisement - Leaderboard (728x90)

A bank fee is money the bank charges you for providing services or maintaining your bank account. Think of it like a service charge - similar to how a restaurant might add a service fee to your bill, banks charge fees for various banking activities.

Some fees are for specific services you choose to use. Others are penalties for certain actions like spending more than you have. Understanding these charges helps you keep more of your money.

What Are Bank Fees?

Bank fees are charges applied to your account for various reasons. They can be monthly charges for maintaining your account, one-time fees for specific services, or penalties for breaking account rules.

These fees are automatically deducted from your account balance. For example, if you have $100 in your account and get charged a $12 monthly fee, your balance drops to $88.

Why Do Banks Charge Fees?

Banks charge fees for several reasons, and understanding why helps explain the different types of charges you might see.

Covering Operating Costs

Running a bank costs money. They need to pay employees, maintain branches and ATMs, develop mobile apps, and provide customer service. Monthly maintenance fees help cover these costs.

Traditional banks with physical branches typically charge higher fees than online banks because operating physical locations is expensive. Online banks can offer lower or no fees since they don't have branch costs.

Encouraging Certain Behaviors

Banks use fees to encourage customers to use accounts responsibly. Overdraft fees (penalties for spending more than you have) discourage people from spending money they don't have. Inactivity fees encourage people to actually use their accounts.

By making certain behaviors expensive, banks push customers toward better account management.

Generating Revenue

Fees are a significant revenue source for banks. In fact, many banks earn billions of dollars annually from customer fees. This is especially true for penalty fees like overdrafts and late payments.

Banks must disclose all fees in your account agreement, so you can see exactly what you might be charged before opening an account.

Common Types of Bank Fees

Monthly Maintenance Fee

This is a regular charge (typically $5-15 per month) just for having an account open. Many banks waive this fee if you meet certain requirements like maintaining a minimum balance or setting up direct deposit.

Example: Your bank charges $12 per month for your checking account. That's $144 per year. If you maintain a $1,500 minimum balance, the fee is waived, saving you $144 annually.

Overdraft Fee

When you spend more money than you have in your account, banks charge an overdraft fee of $25-35 per transaction. These can add up quickly if multiple transactions go through on the same day.

Example: You have $20 in your account and buy coffee ($5), lunch ($15), and groceries ($40). All three overdraw your account. You're charged $35 × 3 = $105 in overdraft fees on top of the $60 you spent.

ATM Fee

Using an ATM that's not in your bank's network typically costs $2-3 from the ATM owner, plus another $2-3 from your own bank. That's $4-6 total just to withdraw your own money.

Example: You withdraw $40 from an out-of-network ATM twice a week. At $5 per withdrawal, that's $520 per year in ATM fees. Using your bank's ATMs would cost $0.

Overdraft Protection Transfer Fee

If you link your savings to your checking for overdraft protection, the bank might charge $10-12 each time they transfer money to cover a shortfall. This is cheaper than a full overdraft fee but still costs money.

Insufficient Funds (NSF) Fee

Similar to an overdraft fee, but charged when a payment is rejected because you don't have enough money. The payment doesn't go through, but you still pay the fee ($25-35).

Wire Transfer Fee

Sending money via wire transfer costs $15-30 for domestic transfers and $35-50 for international transfers. Receiving a wire might also cost $10-15.

Paper Statement Fee

Some banks charge $2-5 per month if you want paper statements mailed instead of viewing them online. Switching to electronic statements is usually free.

Account Closure Fee

Closing your account within 90-180 days of opening it might trigger a $25-50 fee. Banks charge this to discourage people from opening accounts just for sign-up bonuses.

How to Avoid Bank Fees

Most bank fees are avoidable if you understand how they work and manage your account carefully. Here are strategies to keep your money instead of giving it to the bank.

Choose the Right Account

Many banks offer free checking accounts with no monthly fees. Online banks especially tend to have fewer fees than traditional banks. Compare different banks before opening an account.

Look for accounts that match your banking habits. If you rarely keep large balances, avoid accounts with minimum balance requirements.

Meet Fee Waiver Requirements

If your account has a monthly fee, check what you need to do to waive it. Common requirements include maintaining a minimum daily balance, setting up direct deposit of your paycheck, or being under age 25.

Example: Your account charges $10 monthly but waives the fee with $500 minimum balance or one direct deposit per month. Getting your paycheck deposited saves you $120 per year.

Track Your Balance

Knowing how much money you have prevents overdraft fees. Check your account regularly, set up low balance alerts, and keep a buffer of $50-100 in your account as a cushion.

Mobile banking apps make it easy to check your balance before making purchases. Taking 10 seconds to check can save you $35 in overdraft fees.

Use Your Bank's ATMs

Stick to ATMs in your bank's network to avoid fees. Most banks have ATM locators on their apps showing you nearby free ATMs. Some online banks reimburse ATM fees, giving you access to any ATM without charges.

Go Paperless

Switch to electronic statements and online bill pay to avoid paper statement fees and check-ordering fees. Electronic statements are also easier to search and can't get lost in the mail.

Reading Your Fee Schedule

Every bank provides a fee schedule listing all charges. You get this when opening an account, and it's available on the bank's website. This document shows exactly what each fee is and when it applies.

Take time to read this before choosing a bank. Look for accounts with the fewest fees that match your banking needs. For more details on specific charges, check out our guide on common bank fees.

Your Rights Regarding Fees

Banks must clearly disclose all fees before you open an account. They can't surprise you with hidden charges. If your bank changes its fee structure, they must notify you in advance (usually 30-60 days).

You have the right to close your account if you don't like the fees. If you're charged a fee you believe is unfair, call your bank and politely explain the situation. They sometimes waive fees, especially if it's your first time being charged.

Key Takeaways

  • Bank fees are charges for services, account maintenance, or penalties for certain actions
  • Common fees include monthly maintenance ($5-15), overdraft ($25-35), and ATM fees ($2-5)
  • Banks charge fees to cover costs, encourage responsible banking, and generate revenue
  • Most fees are avoidable by choosing the right account and managing it carefully
  • Always read the fee schedule before opening an account
  • Online banks typically charge fewer fees than traditional banks
