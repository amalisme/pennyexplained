Common Bank Fees Explained

Banks charge various fees for account maintenance, transactions, and services. Understanding these common fees helps you avoid unnecessary charges and choose the right banking options for your needs.

The most common fees include monthly maintenance, overdraft, ATM, wire transfer, and returned item fees. Each serves a different purpose and can often be avoided with the right knowledge.

Monthly Maintenance Fee

A monthly maintenance fee is a recurring charge just for having an account. Banks typically charge $5 to $15 per month for checking accounts and sometimes for savings accounts.

For example, a bank might charge $12 monthly for a standard checking account. Over a year, that's $144 just for having the account open.

Most banks waive this fee if you meet certain requirements. Common ways to avoid it include maintaining a minimum daily balance (often $500 to $1,500), setting up direct deposit of at least $500 per month, or being under a certain age (like 25).

Here's a real example: Bank of America's Core Checking charges $12 per month, but they waive it if you have direct deposits of $250 or more, maintain a $1,500 minimum daily balance, or are enrolled as a student under 24.

Overdraft Fee

An overdraft fee is charged when you spend more money than you have in your account. This typically costs $30 to $35 per transaction, and you can be charged multiple times in one day.

Here's how it happens: You have $100 in your account. You buy groceries for $80 (balance: $20). Then you buy gas for $50. You don't have enough money, so the bank covers the $30 difference but charges you a $35 overdraft fee. Your new balance is -$65.

If you make three transactions while overdrawn, you could pay three separate overdraft feesâ€”potentially $105 in fees in a single day.

Some banks limit how many overdraft fees they charge per day (often 3-4 fees maximum). Others charge extended overdraft fees if your account stays negative for multiple days, adding $5 to $10 per day.

Ways to avoid overdraft fees include opting out of overdraft coverage (your card simply declines instead), linking a savings account to cover overdrafts, or signing up for low balance alerts that warn you before you run out of money.

Non-Sufficient Funds (NSF) Fee

An NSF fee is similar to an overdraft fee but occurs when the bank declines a transaction because you don't have enough money. Even though the transaction doesn't go through, you still pay the feeâ€”usually $30 to $35.

For example, you have $50 and try to pay a $100 bill. The bank declines the payment and charges you $35. Now you have $15, the bill isn't paid, and you may also face a late fee from whoever you were trying to pay.

ATM Fees

ATM fees come in two types: fees from your bank and fees from the ATM owner. When you use an ATM outside your bank's network, you typically pay both.

Your bank might charge $2 to $3 for using an out-of-network ATM. The ATM owner also charges a fee, usually $2 to $4. Together, a single withdrawal could cost you $4 to $7 in fees.

If you withdraw $40 from an out-of-network ATM and pay $6 in fees, that's 15% of your withdrawal gone to fees. Withdrawing $20 could mean 30% goes to fees.

To avoid ATM fees, use your bank's ATMs or those in their partner network, get cash back when making purchases at stores (usually free), or choose a bank that reimburses ATM fees.

Wire Transfer Fees

Wire transfer fees are charged when you send money electronically to another bank, especially for large amounts or international transfers. Domestic wire transfers typically cost $15 to $30, while international wires cost $35 to $50.

For example, if you send $5,000 to someone using a wire transfer, your bank might charge $25 for the service. The receiving bank might also charge $10 to $15 to accept the wire.

Alternatives to wire transfers include ACH transfers (usually free but slower), payment apps like Venmo or Zelle (often free for personal use), or bank-to-bank transfers through online banking (typically free).

Paper Statement Fee

Some banks charge $2 to $5 per month if you want paper statements mailed to you instead of receiving electronic statements. Over a year, that's $24 to $60 for something you can get free digitally.

Most people can easily avoid this by opting for electronic statements, which are delivered to your email or available through online banking.

Minimum Balance Fee

A minimum balance fee occurs when your account balance drops below a required amount. This might be $5 to $15 charged monthly or quarterly whenever you're below the minimum.

For example, if your account requires a $300 minimum balance and you drop to $250, you might be charged a $10 fee. This makes your balance even lower ($240), potentially triggering the fee again next month.

Returned Deposit Fee

If you deposit a check that bounces (meaning the check writer didn't have enough money), the bank charges you a returned deposit fee. This is typically $10 to $15.

For example, someone writes you a $200 check. You deposit it and your balance increases by $200. A week later, the check bounces. The bank removes the $200 from your account and charges you a $12 fee. If you already spent that money, you could also face overdraft fees.

Stop Payment Fee

A stop payment fee is charged when you ask the bank to cancel a check you wrote. This typically costs $20 to $35 per request.

You might use this if you lost a check and don't want it cashed, or if you wrote a check but the situation changed. The bank has to monitor for that specific check and prevent it from processing, which is why they charge for the service.

Account Closure Fee

Some banks charge $10 to $50 if you close your account within 90 to 180 days of opening it. This discourages people from opening accounts just for sign-up bonuses.

For example, if you open an account in January and close it in March (within 90 days), you might pay a $25 early closure fee.

Foreign Transaction Fee

When you use your debit card in another country or make a purchase in foreign currency, banks often charge 1% to 3% of the transaction amount.

If you spend $500 while traveling abroad, a 3% fee would cost you $15. Make several purchases and these fees add up quickly.

Some banks and credit unions don't charge foreign transaction fees, which is worth considering if you travel internationally.

Excessive Withdrawal Fee

Savings accounts sometimes charge fees if you make more than six withdrawals per month. The fee is typically $5 to $15 per transaction beyond the limit.

For example, if you make eight withdrawals from savings in one month, the first six are free but you pay $10 each for the seventh and eighth, totaling $20 in fees.

How to Check Your Fees

Your bank's fee schedule is usually available on their website under "Account Information" or "Fees." You can also request a printed copy at any branch or call customer service to ask about specific fees.

Review your monthly statements to see what fees you've actually been charged. They'll be listed in your transaction history with labels like "Monthly Fee," "Overdraft Charge," or "ATM Fee."

Key Takeaways

  • Monthly maintenance fees ($5-$15) can often be waived with direct deposit or minimum balances
  • Overdraft fees ($30-$35 per transaction) happen when you spend more than you have
  • ATM fees ($4-$7 total) occur when using out-of-network machines
  • Wire transfers cost $15-$50 depending on domestic or international
  • Many fees are completely avoidable with awareness and planning
  • Online banks and credit unions typically charge fewer and lower fees
