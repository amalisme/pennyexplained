How Much People Typically Save

Saving 6 min read

Wondering if you're saving enough? Here's how much people typically save at various income levels, plus realistic examples to guide your own savings goals.

Advertisement - Leaderboard (728x90)

Average savings by income (2025 data): Americans save an average of 3-7% of their income, though financial experts recommend 15-20%. Actual savings rates vary significantly by income level, age, and financial goals.

Savings by Income Level

Annual Income Average Savings Rate Monthly Amount Recommended Rate
Under $30,000 2-5% $50-125 5-10%
$30,000-$50,000 4-8% $133-333 10-15%
$50,000-$75,000 6-12% $313-750 15%
$75,000-$100,000 8-15% $625-1,250 15-20%
Over $100,000 10-20%+ $1,250+ 20%+

Real Examples by Age and Income

Example 1: Entry-Level Worker

Profile: Age 24, single, income $35,000/year ($2,917/month after taxes)

Typical savings: $150-200/month (5-7%)

Where it goes:

  • Emergency fund: $100/month
  • 401(k): $88/month (3% to get employer match)
  • Total: $188/month (6.4%)

After 5 years: $11,280 in savings + $5,280 in 401(k) with match = $16,560 total

Example 2: Mid-Career Professional

Profile: Age 35, married, combined income $90,000/year ($6,000/month after taxes)

Typical savings: $750-900/month (12-15%)

Where it goes:

  • Emergency fund (building): $300/month
  • Retirement (both 401ks): $450/month (with employer match)
  • Children's college fund: $100/month
  • Total: $850/month (14%)

After 5 years: $18,000 emergency fund + $27,000 retirement + $6,000 college fund = $51,000 total

Example 3: Established Household

Profile: Age 45, married, combined income $120,000/year ($7,800/month after taxes)

Typical savings: $1,200-1,560/month (15-20%)

Where it goes:

  • Emergency fund (maintained): $0 (already have 6 months)
  • Retirement: $975/month (15% gross income with match)
  • College savings: $250/month
  • House down payment fund: $300/month
  • Total: $1,525/month (19.5%)

Savings by Life Stage

Age 20-30: Focus on foundation

  • Emergency fund priority: Build to $1,000, then 3 months expenses
  • Typical rate: 5-10% of income
  • Main goal: Start retirement savings early for compound growth
  • Average 25-year-old savings: $6,000-$15,000

Age 30-40: Accelerate building

  • Emergency fund: Complete 6 months expenses
  • Typical rate: 10-15% of income
  • Main goals: Max out 401(k) match, save for house, start college funds
  • Average 35-year-old savings: $30,000-$50,000 (excluding home equity)

Age 40-50: Peak earning and saving

  • Typical rate: 15-20% of income
  • Main goals: Max retirement contributions, prepare for college costs
  • Average 45-year-old retirement savings: $150,000-$250,000

Age 50-65: Final push

  • Typical rate: 20-30% of income
  • Main goals: Catch-up contributions, eliminate debt, boost retirement
  • Average 60-year-old retirement savings: $400,000-$700,000

The 50/30/20 Savings Benchmark

Widely recommended budgeting framework:

  • 50% Needs: Essential expenses (housing, food, utilities, transportation)
  • 30% Wants: Discretionary spending (entertainment, dining out, hobbies)
  • 20% Savings: Emergency fund, retirement, goals

Applied to different incomes:

Monthly Income Needs (50%) Wants (30%) Savings (20%)
$2,500 $1,250 $750 $500
$4,000 $2,000 $1,200 $800
$6,000 $3,000 $1,800 $1,200

Why People Save Less Than Recommended

Common reasons for low savings rates:

  • High cost of living: 40% of income may go to housing alone in expensive cities
  • Debt payments: Student loans, credit cards consume 10-30% of income
  • Lifestyle inflation: Spending increases with income
  • No emergency fund: Irregular expenses disrupt savings
  • Lack of automation: Manual saving requires more discipline

How to Increase Your Savings Rate

Gradual increase method:

  1. Start where you are: Even 1-2% is progress
  2. Increase with raises: Save 50% of any raise or bonus
  3. Annual 1% bump: Increase savings rate by 1% each year
  4. Track progress: Review quarterly and adjust

Example progression:

  • Year 1: Save 5% = $2,500 on $50,000 income
  • Year 2: Save 6% = $3,000 on $50,000 income
  • Year 3: Save 7% on $52,000 income = $3,640
  • Year 4: Save 8% on $54,000 income = $4,320
  • Year 5: Save 9% on $56,000 income = $5,040
  • 5-year total: $18,500 saved through gradual increases

Key Takeaways

  • Building savings creates financial security and reduces stress
  • Emergency funds prevent debt by covering unexpected expenses
  • Consistent saving habits compound into significant results over time
  • Starting small and automating savings ensures long-term success
