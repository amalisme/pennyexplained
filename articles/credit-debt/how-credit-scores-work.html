How Credit Scores Work

Credit & Debt 8 min read

Credit scores are calculated using complex formulas that analyze your credit history. Understanding how they work helps you improve yours.

Advertisement - Leaderboard (728x90)

Credit scores aren't random - they're calculated using specific factors from your credit report. The formula weighs different aspects of how you've handled credit over time.

The Five Factors (FICO)

1. Payment History (35%)

This is the biggest factor. It tracks whether you pay bills on time.

  • Late payments hurt your score
  • On-time payments help your score
  • The more recent the late payment, the more it hurts
  • 30+ days late damages more than a few days late

Example: One 30-day late payment can drop your score 60-110 points, depending on your starting score.

2. Credit Utilization (30%)

This measures how much of your available credit you're using.

Formula: (Total balances) ÷ (Total credit limits) × 100

Example: You have two credit cards:

  • Card 1: $500 balance, $2,000 limit
  • Card 2: $1,000 balance, $3,000 limit
  • Total: $1,500 ÷ $5,000 = 30% utilization

Lower is better. Experts recommend keeping it under 30%, with under 10% being ideal. Understanding credit utilization is key to maintaining a good score.

3. Length of Credit History (15%)

How long you've had credit matters:

  • Average age of all accounts
  • Age of oldest account
  • Age of newest account

Longer history = better score. This is why financial experts say not to close old credit cards - it can shorten your credit history.

4. Credit Mix (10%)

Having different types of credit shows you can manage various accounts:

  • Credit cards (revolving)
  • Auto loans (installment)
  • Mortgages (installment)
  • Student loans (installment)

You don't need all types, but having more than just credit cards can help slightly. Different types of debt affect your score in different ways.

5. New Credit (10%)

Opening too many accounts quickly looks risky:

  • Hard inquiries (when you apply for credit)
  • Number of recently opened accounts
  • Time since last account opening

Each hard inquiry can drop your score 5-10 points temporarily. Multiple inquiries in a short time for the same type of loan (like shopping for a mortgage) usually count as just one.

How Scores Are Used

Lenders: They use your score to decide if they'll approve your application and what interest rate to offer.

Insurance companies: Many use credit-based insurance scores to set premiums.

Landlords: Use scores to evaluate rental applications.

Employers: Some check credit for jobs involving money handling (with your permission).

What Doesn't Affect Your Score

  • Your income
  • Your age, race, or gender
  • Where you live
  • Your job or employment history
  • Checking your own score
  • Debit card use

Many people are surprised to learn their income doesn't directly affect their credit score - it's all about how you manage the credit you have, not how much money you make.

Key Takeaways

  • Understanding credit and debt helps you make better financial decisions
  • Your credit score significantly impacts your financial opportunities
  • Managing credit responsibly saves you thousands in interest over time
  • Knowledge is power when it comes to credit and borrowing
