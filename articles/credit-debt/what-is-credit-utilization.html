What is Credit Utilization?

Credit & Debt 5 min read

Credit utilization is the percentage of your available credit that you're currently using. It's one of the biggest factors affecting your credit score.

Advertisement - Leaderboard (728x90)

Credit utilization shows lenders how much of your available credit you're using. It's expressed as a percentage and lower is better for your score.

How to Calculate Credit Utilization

Formula: (Total Credit Card Balances) ÷ (Total Credit Limits) × 100

Example:

  • Card 1: $800 balance / $2,000 limit
  • Card 2: $450 balance / $3,000 limit
  • Card 3: $250 balance / $5,000 limit

Total balances: $1,500
Total limits: $10,000
Utilization: $1,500 ÷ $10,000 = 15%

Why It Matters

Credit utilization makes up about 30% of your credit score - it's the second-biggest factor after payment history.

High utilization suggests you might be overextended financially. Lenders see this as risky because you're relying heavily on credit.

Utilization Guidelines

  • Under 10%: Excellent - optimal for your score
  • 10-30%: Good - acceptable range
  • 30-50%: Fair - starts to hurt your score
  • 50-75%: Poor - significant score damage
  • Over 75%: Very Poor - major score impact

Per-Card vs Overall Utilization

Credit scoring models look at both:

Overall utilization: Your total balances across all cards divided by total limits.

Per-card utilization: The balance on each individual card divided by that card's limit.

Having one card maxed out hurts your score even if your overall utilization is low.

Example:

  • Card 1: $2,000 / $2,000 limit = 100% (bad!)
  • Card 2: $0 / $8,000 limit = 0%
  • Overall: $2,000 / $10,000 = 20% (looks okay)

Despite 20% overall utilization, the maxed card damages your score.

How to Lower Your Utilization

Pay down balances: The most direct way. Every payment reduces your utilization and helps you manage your debt better.

Pay twice a month: Instead of one monthly payment, pay mid-month too. This keeps your reported balance lower.

Request credit limit increases: Higher limits with the same balance = lower utilization percentage.

Don't close unused cards: Closing cards reduces your total available credit, which increases utilization.

When Utilization Is Reported

Credit card companies report your balance on your statement date - not your due date. This means your score reflects whatever balance you had when your statement generated.

Strategy: Pay down your balance before your statement closes to show a lower utilization, even if you plan to pay in full later. This is one of many factors covered in our guide on what affects credit scores.

Understanding credit utilization is especially important if you're trying to avoid accumulating bad debt through high-interest credit card balances.

Key Takeaways

  • Understanding credit and debt helps you make better financial decisions
  • Your credit score significantly impacts your financial opportunities
  • Managing credit responsibly saves you thousands in interest over time
  • Knowledge is power when it comes to credit and borrowing
