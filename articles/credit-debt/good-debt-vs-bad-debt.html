Good Debt vs Bad Debt

Credit & Debt 6 min read

Not all debt is equal. Some debt can help build wealth (good debt), while other debt just costs you money (bad debt). Understanding the difference helps you make better borrowing decisions.

The concept of "good" versus "bad" debt helps you evaluate whether taking on debt makes financial sense. It's about whether the debt helps or hurts your financial future.

What Makes Debt "Good"?

Good debt generally has these characteristics:

  • Helps you build wealth or increase income
  • Has relatively low interest rates
  • Finances an appreciating asset (gains value over time)
  • Is an investment in your future

Examples of Good Debt

Mortgages

Borrowing to buy a home can be good debt because:

  • Homes often appreciate in value over time
  • You build equity as you pay down the loan
  • Mortgage interest rates are typically lower than other debt
  • You need somewhere to live anyway

Example: You buy a $300,000 house with a mortgage. Over 10 years, you pay down $80,000 of the loan, and the house appreciates to $350,000. Your net worth increased while you lived there.

Student Loans

Education debt can be good because:

  • Education typically increases earning potential
  • Federal student loans have relatively low rates
  • It's an investment in yourself

Note: This assumes the degree leads to higher income. A $100,000 loan for a degree in a field with $30,000 starting salaries might not be good debt.

Business Loans

Borrowing to start or grow a business can be good if:

  • The business generates profit greater than the interest cost
  • It helps you build a valuable asset
  • You have a solid business plan

What Makes Debt "Bad"?

Bad debt typically has these characteristics:

  • Finances depreciating assets (loses value)
  • Has high interest rates
  • Pays for consumption, not investment
  • Doesn't help build wealth

Examples of Bad Debt

Credit Card Debt

Carrying credit card balances is often bad because:

  • Very high interest rates (15-25%+)
  • Usually spent on consumables or depreciating items
  • Interest compounds quickly
  • Doesn't build wealth

Example: Charging a $2,000 vacation on a credit card at 20% APR and taking 2 years to pay it off costs you $400+ in interest. You're paying extra for a memory.

Auto Loans (Sometimes)

Car loans can be problematic because:

  • Cars depreciate rapidly (lose value)
  • A $30,000 car might be worth $20,000 after 3 years
  • You're paying interest on a depreciating asset

Note: If you need a car for work and can't afford to pay cash, it might be necessary debt. But it's still not "good" debt in the traditional sense.

Payday Loans

Almost always bad debt:

  • Extremely high interest rates (often 400%+ APR)
  • Short repayment periods create debt cycles
  • Used for emergency expenses, not investments

Consumer Debt for Wants

Borrowing to buy things you don't need:

  • Furniture on store credit at high rates
  • Electronics financed through retail cards
  • Luxury items bought on credit

The Gray Area

Some debt doesn't fit neatly into good or bad categories:

Small business credit cards: High interest (bad) but might be necessary to smooth cash flow in a profitable business (good outcome).

Medical debt: Often unavoidable and doesn't build wealth, but addressing health issues is necessary.

0% promotional financing: No interest (good) but tempts overspending (potentially bad).

The Key Question

Before taking on debt, ask: "Will this help me build wealth or increase my income more than the cost of the debt?"

If yes → Potentially good debt
If no → Probably bad debt

Remember: Even "good" debt can become bad if you borrow more than you can reasonably repay or if the investment doesn't pan out as expected. Understanding what affects your credit score also helps you manage any debt more effectively.

Key Takeaways

  • Your credit score significantly impacts your financial opportunities

  • Knowledge is power when it comes to credit and borrowing
