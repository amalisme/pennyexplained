What is Interest?

Credit & Debt 6 min read

Interest is the cost of borrowing money, or the reward for saving it. It's expressed as a percentage and affects how much you pay or earn.

Advertisement - Leaderboard (728x90)

Interest is extra money paid on top of the original amount borrowed or saved. When you borrow, you pay interest. When you save or invest, you earn interest.

Interest on Loans (You Pay)

When you borrow money, the lender charges interest as their profit for letting you use their money.

Example: You borrow $1,000 at 10% annual interest for one year. At the end of the year, you owe:

  • Original amount (principal): $1,000
  • Interest: $100 (10% of $1,000)
  • Total owed: $1,100

Interest on Savings (You Earn)

When you save money in a bank account, the bank pays you interest for letting them use your money.

Example: You deposit $1,000 in a savings account with 2% annual interest. After one year:

  • Original deposit: $1,000
  • Interest earned: $20 (2% of $1,000)
  • Total balance: $1,020

How Interest Rates Work

Interest rates are shown as percentages per year (annual percentage rate, or APR).

Higher rates mean:

  • More expensive loans (you pay more)
  • Better returns on savings (you earn more)

Lower rates mean:

  • Cheaper loans (you pay less)
  • Lower returns on savings (you earn less)

Simple vs Compound Interest

Simple Interest

Calculated only on the original amount (principal).

Example: $1,000 at 5% simple interest for 3 years:

  • Year 1: $1,000 × 5% = $50
  • Year 2: $1,000 × 5% = $50
  • Year 3: $1,000 × 5% = $50
  • Total interest: $150

Compound Interest

Calculated on the principal plus any previously earned interest. Your money grows faster.

Example: $1,000 at 5% compound interest for 3 years:

  • Year 1: $1,000 × 5% = $50 (balance: $1,050)
  • Year 2: $1,050 × 5% = $52.50 (balance: $1,102.50)
  • Year 3: $1,102.50 × 5% = $55.13 (balance: $1,157.63)
  • Total interest: $157.63

Compound interest earned you $7.63 more than simple interest on the same amount.

Interest on Credit Cards

Credit cards charge interest on balances you carry past the due date.

Example: You charge $1,000 on a card with 18% APR and make only minimum payments:

  • Monthly interest rate: 18% ÷ 12 = 1.5%
  • First month interest: $1,000 × 1.5% = $15
  • New balance: $1,015

This compounds monthly. If you only pay minimums, that $1,000 could take years to pay off and cost hundreds in interest.

Why Interest Exists

For lenders: Interest is their profit and compensation for risk. They're taking a chance that you might not repay.

For savers: Interest is your reward for letting the bank use your money to make loans to others.
