What is a Credit Score?

Credit & Debt 6 min read

A credit score is a three-digit number (300-850) that represents how trustworthy you are with borrowed money. Higher scores mean better credit.

Advertisement - Leaderboard (728x90)

Your credit score is like a report card for how well you handle debt and credit. Lenders use this number to quickly decide if they should lend you money and at what interest rate.

The Credit Score Range

Credit scores range from 300 to 850:

  • 800-850: Exceptional - Best rates and terms
  • 740-799: Very Good - Great rates
  • 670-739: Good - Decent rates
  • 580-669: Fair - Higher rates, harder to get approved
  • 300-579: Poor - Difficult to get credit, very high rates

What Your Score Affects

Your credit score impacts many areas of your financial life:

Interest rates: The difference between a 3% and 5% interest rate on a $300,000 mortgage is about $125,000 over 30 years. That's how much a good score can save you.

Credit approval: Higher scores make it easier to get approved for credit cards, loans, and mortgages. Lower scores lead to more rejections.

Insurance premiums: Many insurance companies use credit scores to set rates. Better credit can mean lower insurance costs.

Housing: Landlords check credit scores. A low score might mean paying a higher security deposit or getting rejected for an apartment.

Utilities and phones: Companies may require deposits if your score is low.

Common Credit Scoring Models

FICO Score

Used by 90% of lenders. This is the most common score you'll encounter when applying for loans. To understand how credit scores work, it helps to know the formula behind FICO.

VantageScore

Created by the three major credit bureaus. Less commonly used by lenders but often seen in free credit monitoring services.

Example Impact

Let's say you're buying a $25,000 car with a 5-year loan:

  • Score 760+: 4% interest = $460/month, $2,600 total interest
  • Score 660: 7% interest = $495/month, $4,700 total interest
  • Score 580: 12% interest = $556/month, $8,360 total interest

The difference between excellent and poor credit costs you $5,760 on just this one loan. That's why your credit score matters.

Checking Your Score

You can check your credit score through:

  • Your credit card company (many offer free scores)
  • Free credit monitoring services
  • Annual credit reports (doesn't show score but shows history)
  • Banks (some provide scores to customers)

Checking your own score doesn't hurt it. Only applications for new credit (hard inquiries) affect your score. To learn more about the specific factors that influence your score, check out our guide on what affects credit scores.

Key Takeaways

  • Understanding credit and debt helps you make better financial decisions
  • Your credit score significantly impacts your financial opportunities
  • Managing credit responsibly saves you thousands in interest over time
  • Knowledge is power when it comes to credit and borrowing
