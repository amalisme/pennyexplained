What Affects a Credit Score?

Credit & Debt 7 min read

Many actions affect your credit score - some help, some hurt. Knowing what impacts your score helps you make better financial decisions.

Advertisement - Leaderboard (728x90)

Your credit score changes based on how you use credit. Every action related to borrowing money can potentially help or hurt your score.

Things That Help Your Score

Paying On Time, Every Time

This is the #1 thing you can do for your credit. Paying all bills by their due dates builds a strong payment history.

Impact: Payment history is 35% of your score. Consistent on-time payments gradually increase your score.

Keeping Balances Low

Using less than 30% (ideally less than 10%) of your available credit helps significantly. This is measured by your credit utilization ratio.

Example: If you have $10,000 in total credit limits, try to keep your total balances under $3,000, preferably under $1,000.

Keeping Old Accounts Open

Older accounts increase your average account age, which helps your score. Don't close your oldest credit cards unless they have high annual fees.

Having a Mix of Credit Types

A combination of credit cards and installment loans (like a car loan) shows you can handle different types of credit.

Limiting New Applications

Space out credit applications. Too many at once makes you look desperate for credit.

Things That Hurt Your Score

Late Payments

Even one late payment can damage your score significantly.

  • 30 days late: Major impact, can drop score 60-110 points
  • 60 days late: More severe impact
  • 90+ days late: Serious damage

High Credit Utilization

Using most of your available credit suggests financial stress.

Example: If you have a $5,000 limit and consistently carry a $4,500 balance (90% utilization), your score will suffer.

Closing Old Accounts

This can hurt in two ways:

  • Reduces your total available credit (increases utilization)
  • May lower your average account age

Maxing Out Credit Cards

Using 100% of your limit, even if you pay it off monthly, can temporarily hurt your score since balances are reported on a specific day each month.

Collections and Charge-offs

Accounts sent to collections or charged off (written off as uncollectible) severely damage your score and stay on your report for 7 years. These negative marks often result from carrying too much debt.

Bankruptcy

The most damaging event for credit scores. Can drop scores 200+ points and stays on your report for 7-10 years.

Applying for Multiple Credit Cards Quickly

Each application is a "hard inquiry" that can drop your score 5-10 points. Multiple inquiries in a short time compound the damage.

How Fast Changes Happen

Quick drops: Your score can drop significantly in a single month from one late payment or maxed card.

Slow increases: Building good credit takes time. Consistent on-time payments improve your score gradually over months and years.

Recovery time:

  • Hard inquiry: 3-12 months
  • 30-day late payment: 18-24 months to recover fully
  • Collection: 7 years to fall off report
  • Bankruptcy: 7-10 years to fall off report

Understanding these factors helps you avoid common mistakes. Many of the misconceptions people have about credit are addressed in our article on credit score myths.

Key Takeaways

  • Understanding credit and debt helps you make better financial decisions
  • Your credit score significantly impacts your financial opportunities
  • Managing credit responsibly saves you thousands in interest over time
  • Knowledge is power when it comes to credit and borrowing
