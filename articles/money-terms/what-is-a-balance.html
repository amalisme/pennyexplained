What is a Balance?

5 min read

A balance is the amount of money in an account or the amount you owe on a credit card or loan. For bank accounts, it's how much money you have. For credit cards and loans, it's how much you owe.

Think of it as a snapshot of where you stand financially with that account at a specific moment in time.

Bank Account Balances

Your checking or savings account balance shows how much money you have in that account. This changes when you deposit money, withdraw cash, make purchases, or earn interest.

If you have $1,500 in your checking account, that's your balance. Spend $200 on groceries, and your new balance is $1,300. Deposit your $2,000 paycheck, and your balance becomes $3,300.

Credit Card Balances

Your credit card balance is the total amount you owe to the credit card company. This includes purchases, fees, and interest charges that haven't been paid yet.

Let's say you start a month with a $500 balance. You charge $300 in purchases and make a $200 payment. Your new balance would be $600 ($500 + $300 - $200).

Different Types of Balances

Current balance shows what you owe right now, including all transactions that have processed. Available balance shows how much you can actually spend or withdraw at this moment.

For credit cards, your current balance might be $2,000. But if you have pending transactions of $300 that haven't processed yet, your available credit is lower than what your current balance suggests.

Statement balance is the amount you owed at the end of your last billing cycle. This is what appears on your monthly statement and what you should pay to avoid interest.

Available Balance vs Current Balance

In a checking account, these can differ. Your current balance might show $800, but if you wrote a check for $200 that hasn't cleared yet, your available balance might only be $600.

The available balance is usually a safer number to rely on for spending decisions. It accounts for pending transactions that will soon come out of your account.

How Balances Change

Bank account balances go up when you deposit money or earn interest. They go down when you make purchases, pay bills, withdraw cash, or pay fees.

Credit card balances go up when you make purchases or get charged fees and interest. They go down when you make payments. The balance resets each billing cycle but continues from any unpaid amount.

Loan Balances

For loans, your balance is the remaining amount you owe. This is also called your principal balance. It decreases as you make payments that go toward the principal.

If you took out a $20,000 car loan and have made payments for a year, your balance might now be $16,500. That's what you still owe on the loan.

Each payment typically includes both principal and interest. The principal portion reduces your balance. The interest portion is the cost of borrowing but doesn't reduce the balance.

Statement Balance vs Current Balance

Your statement balance is what you owed when the billing cycle ended. Your current balance is what you owe right now, which includes new charges since the statement.

Your March statement might show a balance of $1,200. That's your statement balance. If you've charged $400 more in April, your current balance is $1,600.

To avoid interest on a credit card, you need to pay at least the statement balance by the due date, not necessarily the current balance.

Checking Your Balance

You can check your bank account balance through online banking, mobile apps, ATMs, or by calling your bank. Most people check balances through apps because it's instant and convenient.

Credit card balances are available on your monthly statement, through online account access, or via mobile apps. Many apps send notifications when your balance reaches certain amounts.

Why Balances Matter

Knowing your bank account balance helps prevent overdrafts. If you try to spend more than your available balance, the bank might decline the transaction or charge you an overdraft fee, typically around $35.

Knowing your credit card balance helps you manage debt and avoid going over your credit limit. High credit card balances can also hurt your credit score through something called credit utilization.

Negative Balances

A negative bank account balance means you've spent more than you had. You owe the bank money. This usually happens from overdrafts and comes with fees.

A negative credit card balance means the card company owes you money. This can happen if you return a purchase after paying your bill, or if you overpay. The credit stays on your account for future purchases, or you can request a refund.

Maintaining Healthy Balances

For bank accounts, keeping a buffer above zero prevents overdraft fees. Many people try to maintain a minimum cushion, like $100 or $500, to avoid accidentally going negative.

For credit cards, keeping balances low relative to your credit limit is ideal. Using less than 30% of your available credit helps your credit score. On a card with a $5,000 limit, that means keeping balances under $1,500.

Key Takeaways

  • Balance is the amount in your account (bank) or the amount you owe (credit/loans)
  • Current balance shows what you have or owe right now
  • Available balance shows what you can actually spend or withdraw
  • Statement balance is what you owed at the end of your billing cycle
  • Balances change with deposits, withdrawals, purchases, and payments
  • Checking balances regularly helps avoid overdrafts and manage debt
  • Keeping credit card balances low (under 30% of limit) helps your credit score
