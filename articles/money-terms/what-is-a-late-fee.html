What is a Late Fee?

5 min read

A late fee is a charge you pay when you don't make a payment by its due date. Credit cards, loans, utilities, and rent can all charge late fees if you miss the deadline. It's a penalty for paying after the required date.

Think of it like a parking ticket for your bills. Just as you get fined for parking too long in a spot, you get charged a fee for paying too late.

How Late Fees Work

Every bill has a due date when payment must be received. If the company doesn't receive at least the minimum payment by that date, they charge you a late fee.

The fee gets added to your balance. So if you owe $500 on a credit card and miss the payment, you might get charged a $30 late fee. Now you owe $530.

Common Late Fee Amounts

Credit card late fees are typically $30 to $41, depending on your payment history. The first time you're late, the fee might be around $30. If you're late again within six months, it can jump to $41.

Personal loan late fees vary widely. Some lenders charge a flat fee like $25 or $50. Others charge a percentage of your missed payment, such as 5% of the payment amount.

Mortgage late fees are often around 4% to 5% of your monthly payment. On a $1,500 mortgage payment, that could be $60 to $75 in late fees.

When Late Fees Are Charged

Most companies charge late fees if payment isn't received by the due date. Some give you a grace period of a few days, but many don't.

Credit cards typically charge late fees the day after your due date if they haven't received the minimum payment. Some have a cutoff time, like 5 PM on the due date.

Rent late fees often kick in after a grace period. Your lease might say rent is due on the 1st but late fees start on the 5th if you haven't paid.

Late Fee Regulations

Federal law limits how much credit card companies can charge in late fees. The fee can't exceed the minimum payment you owed. So if your minimum payment was $25, the late fee can't be more than $25.

There are also maximum dollar amounts set by regulators. These amounts can change, but they're designed to prevent excessive fees.

Multiple Late Fees

You can only be charged one late fee per billing cycle on credit cards. Even if you're several months behind, they can't charge you multiple late fees for the same payment.

However, if you miss payments in consecutive months, you get charged a late fee each month. Miss payment in March, April, and May, and you'll get three separate late fees.

Late Fees and Your Credit Score

The late fee itself doesn't hurt your credit score. But the late payment that caused the fee can damage your score if it's reported to credit bureaus.

Credit card companies typically report late payments to credit bureaus after you're 30 days late. A payment 5 days late gets you a late fee, but probably won't appear on your credit report.

Once a late payment is reported, it can stay on your credit report for seven years and significantly lower your credit score.

Other Consequences of Late Payments

Beyond the late fee, being late can trigger other penalties. Your credit card company might raise your interest rate to a penalty APR, which can be 29.99% or higher.

You might lose promotional interest rates. If you had 0% APR for 18 months and miss a payment, that rate could disappear and be replaced with the regular rate.

Some companies report late payments to collection agencies if you're very late. This can lead to phone calls, letters, and additional fees.

How to Avoid Late Fees

Setting up automatic payments ensures you never miss a due date. You can set them for the minimum payment, full balance, or a specific amount.

Calendar reminders a few days before the due date give you time to make the payment. Many banking apps let you schedule payments in advance.

Some companies send email or text alerts before your due date. Signing up for these notifications helps you remember to pay on time.

Getting Late Fees Waived

If you've been a good customer with a history of on-time payments, companies will often waive a late fee if you ask. This usually works for your first late payment.

Call customer service and explain the situation. Many will remove the fee as a courtesy, especially if you make the payment while on the phone with them.

This typically works once or occasionally, not repeatedly. Companies are more willing to help customers who rarely miss payments.

Late Fees on Different Bills

Utility late fees vary by company and state. They might be a flat fee like $5 to $20, or a percentage of your bill. Some utilities charge interest on overdue balances instead of or in addition to late fees.

Cell phone late fees are common and usually range from $5 to $15. Some companies suspend service after a certain number of days late.

Streaming services and subscriptions typically don't charge late fees. Instead, they cancel your service if your payment method fails.

Key Takeaways

  • A late fee is charged when you don't pay a bill by its due date
  • Credit card late fees typically range from $30 to $41
  • Late fees are added to your balance, increasing what you owe
  • Payments 30+ days late can be reported to credit bureaus and hurt your credit score
  • You can often get your first late fee waived by calling customer service
  • Automatic payments prevent late fees by ensuring on-time payment
  • Late payments can also trigger penalty interest rates and lost promotional rates
