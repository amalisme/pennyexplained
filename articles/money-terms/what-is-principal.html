What is Principal?

5 min read

Principal is the original amount of money you borrow or invest, before any interest is added. When you take out a loan, the principal is how much you actually borrowed. When you invest money, the principal is how much you put in.

Think of principal as the starting point. If you borrow $10,000 for a car, that $10,000 is your principal. Any interest you pay on top of that is separate from the principal.

Principal in Loans

When you take out a loan, the principal is the amount the lender gives you. This is different from the total you'll pay back, which includes interest.

For example, you borrow $5,000 for a personal loan. The $5,000 is your principal. Over time, you might pay back $5,750 total. The $5,000 is principal, and the extra $750 is interest.

How Loan Payments Work

Each loan payment typically includes two parts: principal and interest. Part of your payment reduces the principal you owe. The other part pays the interest charges.

Early in a loan, most of your payment goes toward interest. Later on, more of your payment goes toward principal. This is how loans are structured.

On a $200,000 mortgage, your first payment might split like this: $200 toward principal and $800 toward interest. Years later, a payment might be $700 principal and $300 interest.

Principal in Savings and Investments

When you save or invest money, your principal is the amount you put in. Any earnings from interest or investment gains are separate from your principal.

If you deposit $1,000 into a savings account, that $1,000 is your principal. If you earn $30 in interest over a year, you now have $1,030 total. Your principal is still $1,000, and you have $30 in interest earnings.

Why Principal Matters

Understanding principal helps you see how much you actually owe or have saved. It separates the base amount from additional costs or earnings.

This matters for budgeting and planning. If you know your loan principal is $15,000, you understand the actual debt amount. The interest is the cost of borrowing that money.

Paying Down Principal Faster

Making extra payments toward principal reduces what you owe. Since interest is calculated on your remaining principal, lowering it means paying less interest overall.

Let's say you owe $8,000 on a loan and make an extra $100 principal payment. Your balance drops to $7,900. Next month's interest is calculated on $7,900 instead of $8,000, saving you money.

On a $300,000 mortgage at 6% interest, paying an extra $200 toward principal each month could save you over $70,000 in interest and pay off the loan years earlier.

Principal and Interest Statements

Loan statements show your principal balance separately from interest charges. The principal balance is what you still owe on the original borrowed amount.

Each month, you'll see how much of your payment went to principal versus interest. Over time, you'll watch your principal balance decrease.

Credit Card Principal

With credit cards, principal works a bit differently. The principal is your current balance before new interest is added. When you make purchases, those amounts add to your principal.

If you have a $1,500 credit card balance and make a $100 payment, about $25 might go to interest and $75 to principal. Your new principal balance would be $1,425.

Principal-Only Payments

Some loans let you make principal-only payments. These payments go entirely toward reducing your loan principal, not toward interest.

These payments are usually in addition to your regular payment. They help you pay off the loan faster and save on total interest costs.

Real-World Principal Examples

Student loans often start with a principal amount of $20,000 to $50,000 or more. This is the total you borrowed for school. Interest adds up over time, but the principal is the original borrowed amount.

Car loans typically have principals of $15,000 to $40,000. A $25,000 car loan at 5% for five years means your principal is $25,000. You'll pay about $3,300 in interest on top of that.

If you invest $3,000 in a retirement account, that's your principal investment. If it grows to $3,500 over a year, your principal is still $3,000 and your gain is $500.

Principal Protection

When investing, some products offer principal protection. This means your original investment amount is guaranteed, even if the investment loses value.

These products ensure you won't lose your principal, though you might not earn any returns either. Regular investments can lose principal value if the market goes down.

Key Takeaways

  • Principal is the original amount borrowed or invested, before interest
  • For loans, it's how much you borrowed; for savings, it's how much you put in
  • Loan payments include both principal and interest portions
  • Paying extra toward principal saves money on total interest
  • Your principal balance decreases as you make loan payments
  • On a $10,000 loan, the $10,000 is principal; any interest is extra
  • Understanding principal helps you track your actual debt or savings amount
