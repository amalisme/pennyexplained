What is Inflation?

Money Terms 6 min read

Inflation is the rate at which prices increase over time. As inflation rises, each dollar buys less than it did before, reducing your money's purchasing power.

Advertisement - Leaderboard (728x90)

Inflation Definition: Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services rises over time, causing purchasing power of currency to fall. It's measured as an annual percentage and means that money loses value - what cost $100 last year might cost $103 this year at 3% inflation. The Federal Reserve targets 2% annual inflation as healthy for the economy.

Simple Explanation

Inflation means things get more expensive over time. The money in your wallet doesn't change, but what it can buy does.

Example:

  • 2000: Gallon of milk costs $2.50
  • 2010: Same gallon costs $3.00
  • 2020: Same gallon costs $3.50
  • 2025: Same gallon costs $4.00

The milk hasn't changed, but inflation has made it 60% more expensive over 25 years.

How Inflation Is Measured

Consumer Price Index (CPI): Most common inflation measure.

How it works:

  • Government tracks prices of ~80,000 consumer items
  • Creates "basket" of common purchases (food, housing, transportation, etc.)
  • Compares basket cost to previous year
  • Percentage increase = inflation rate

Example:

  • Basket cost 2024: $50,000
  • Basket cost 2025: $51,500
  • Inflation rate: ($51,500 - $50,000) ÷ $50,000 = 3%

Historical Inflation Rates (US)

Period Average Annual Inflation Notable Events
1970s 7.1% Oil crisis, peaked at 13.5% in 1980
1980s 5.1% High but declining
1990s 3.0% Stable economic period
2000s 2.6% Generally moderate
2010-2019 1.8% Below Fed target after recession
2020-2023 4.7% Pandemic disruption, peaked at 9.1% in 2022
2024-2025 ~3.0% Declining toward Fed target

Historical average (1926-2025): ~3.0% annually

Impact of Inflation on Purchasing Power

What $100 buys over time at 3% annual inflation:

Years Purchasing Power Loss
0 (today) $100.00 0%
10 $74.41 26%
20 $55.37 45%
30 $41.20 59%
40 $30.66 69%

Key insight: At 3% inflation, money loses half its purchasing power every 24 years.

Real-World Inflation Examples

Price changes over 25 years (1999-2024):

  • Gasoline: $1.19/gallon → $3.50/gallon (+194%)
  • House (median): $161,000 → $420,000 (+161%)
  • New car (average): $21,000 → $48,000 (+129%)
  • College tuition (4-year public): $3,600/year → $11,260/year (+213%)
  • Dozen eggs: $0.96 → $3.00 (+213%)
  • Movie ticket: $5.08 → $11.00 (+117%)

Items that got cheaper:

  • TV (50-inch): $3,000 → $400 (-87%) - technology improves
  • Computer: $2,000 → $500 (-75%) - better and cheaper

How to Protect Against Inflation

Inflation protection strategies:

1. Invest in Assets That Grow

  • Stocks: Historical average return ~10% beats 3% inflation
  • Real estate: Property values typically rise with inflation
  • I-Bonds: Government bonds that adjust with inflation

Example: $10,000 in stocks growing at 10% annually:

  • After 20 years: $67,275
  • After accounting for 3% inflation: $37,312 in today's dollars
  • Still 3.7x better than holding cash

2. Avoid Holding Excess Cash

  • Emergency fund in high-yield savings (4-5%) at least keeps pace
  • Extra cash should be invested, not sitting idle
  • Cash loses 3% purchasing power yearly to inflation

3. Increase Income with Inflation

  • Negotiate raises that match or exceed inflation
  • Cost-of-living adjustments (COLA) in employment
  • If inflation is 5% but you get 3% raise, you're losing 2% purchasing power

4. Lock in Low Rates

  • Fixed-rate mortgages protect against rising housing costs
  • Long-term contracts at current prices

Types of Inflation

Mild Inflation (2-3% annually):

  • Considered healthy for economy
  • Encourages spending and investment
  • Federal Reserve's target: 2%

Moderate Inflation (3-10% annually):

  • Causes some economic concern
  • Erodes purchasing power noticeably
  • Requires active financial planning

High Inflation (10%+ annually):

  • Serious economic problem
  • Rapidly destroys purchasing power
  • Seen in 1970s-early 1980s in US

Hyperinflation (50%+ monthly):

  • Extreme currency collapse
  • Examples: Zimbabwe 2008, Venezuela 2016-2020
  • Money becomes nearly worthless

Deflation (negative inflation):

  • Prices decrease over time
  • Sounds good but usually indicates economic recession
  • People delay purchases expecting lower prices
  • Can spiral into depression

Bottom line: Inflation is normal and expected. The key is ensuring your income and investments grow faster than inflation to maintain and build purchasing power over time.
