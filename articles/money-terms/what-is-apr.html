What is APR?

Money Terms 5 min read

APR stands for Annual Percentage Rate. It's the yearly cost of borrowing money, including interest and fees, expressed as a percentage.

Advertisement - Leaderboard (728x90)

APR Definition: APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the total yearly cost of borrowing money expressed as a percentage. It includes the interest rate plus additional fees and costs associated with the loan. APR provides a standardized way to compare the true cost of different loans or credit products.

Simple Explanation

APR answers the question: "What will this loan cost me per year in total?"

Components of APR:

  • Interest rate: The base cost of borrowing
  • Fees: Origination fees, processing fees, closing costs
  • Annual calculation: Combined into one yearly percentage

Why it matters: A 5% interest rate with $2,000 in fees might have a 6.5% APR. The APR shows the true cost.

APR vs Interest Rate

Aspect Interest Rate APR
What it includes Interest only Interest + fees + costs
Which is higher Lower number Higher number (includes more)
Best for comparison No (incomplete picture) Yes (total cost)
Example 5.0% 5.8%

Example: Mortgage advertised at 5% interest but APR is 5.8% due to $3,000 in fees spread over loan term.

How APR Affects Costs

$10,000 loan over 5 years at different APRs:

  • 5% APR: Monthly payment $188, total paid $11,322, interest $1,322
  • 10% APR: Monthly payment $212, total paid $12,748, interest $2,748
  • 15% APR: Monthly payment $238, total paid $14,273, interest $4,273
  • 20% APR: Monthly payment $265, total paid $15,899, interest $5,899

Impact of APR: The difference between 5% and 20% APR costs you $4,577 extra (3.5x more interest). This is why managing debt wisely matters so much.

Credit Card APR

Credit cards typically have variable APR based on:

  • Prime rate: Base rate (currently ~8.5%) set by Federal Reserve
  • Credit score margin: Additional percentage based on creditworthiness
  • Calculation: Prime rate + margin = Your APR

Your APR on credit cards directly affects how much it costs to use credit.

Example:

  • Prime rate: 8.5%
  • Your margin (good credit): +7.5%
  • Your APR: 16%

Credit card APR types:

  • Purchase APR: Interest on purchases (if balance carried)
  • Cash advance APR: Usually higher (20-25%), starts immediately
  • Penalty APR: Up to 29.99% after late payments
  • Promotional APR: 0% for 12-18 months on some cards

Using APR to Compare

When comparing loans, always use APR, not interest rate:

Example - Two mortgage offers:

Lender A:

  • Interest rate: 5.5%
  • Closing costs: $5,000
  • APR: 5.8%

Lender B:

  • Interest rate: 5.75%
  • Closing costs: $2,000
  • APR: 5.9%

Best choice: Lender A has lower APR despite higher closing costs, making it cheaper overall. Understanding APR versus APY helps you make informed decisions about both borrowing and saving.

How to Lower Your APR

Strategies to get better APR:

  1. Improve credit score: Each 20-point increase can reduce APR by 0.25%-0.5%. Your credit score directly affects your borrowing costs.
  2. Shop multiple lenders: Rates vary significantly between institutions
  3. Larger down payment: Reduces risk for lender, may lower APR
  4. Shorter loan term: 15-year mortgage vs. 30-year typically saves 0.5%-1.0% APR
  5. Use collateral: Secured loans have lower APR than unsecured
  6. Ask for rate reduction: Good payment history = negotiating power

Impact example: Improving credit score from 650 to 750 on $250,000 mortgage could reduce APR from 6.5% to 5.5%, saving $145/month or $52,000 over 30 years.
