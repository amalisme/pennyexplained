What is a Due Date?

5 min read

A due date is the deadline for when a payment must be made. It's the last day you can pay a bill without being considered late. Credit cards, loans, rent, and utilities all have due dates that tell you when payment is expected.

Think of it like a deadline for a school project. Just as you need to turn in homework by a certain date, you need to pay bills by their due date to avoid penalties.

How Due Dates Work

Companies set due dates to ensure regular, predictable payments. They tell you exactly when they need to receive your money. Miss that date, and you typically face late fees or other consequences.

For credit cards, the due date is usually 21 to 25 days after your billing cycle ends. If your statement is created on March 15, your due date might be April 10. You have about 25 days to make at least the minimum payment.

Finding Your Due Date

Your due date appears prominently on your statement, both paper and online. Credit card companies list it on the first page, usually near your balance and minimum payment.

You can also find due dates in your online account, mobile app, or by calling customer service. Most companies send reminder emails or texts a few days before the due date.

When Payment Must Arrive

The due date is when the company must receive your payment, not when you send it. If you mail a check on the due date, it arrives too late. You need to account for processing time.

Online payments made by a certain time on the due date (often 5 PM or midnight) usually count as on-time. But check your company's specific rules, as cutoff times vary.

Some companies consider a payment on-time if it's postmarked by the due date, even if it arrives later. However, this is less common with credit cards.

Different Types of Due Dates

Credit card due dates are fixed each month. If your due date is the 15th, it's the 15th every month. This makes it easier to plan and remember.

Loan due dates work similarly. Your mortgage, car loan, or student loan payment is typically due on the same day each month, like the 1st or the 15th.

Utility bills might have variable due dates based on when your bill is generated. Your electric bill might be due 20 days after it's issued, so the date moves slightly each month.

Grace Periods

Some companies offer grace periods, which is extra time after the due date before they charge a late fee or report you as late. This varies widely by company and bill type.

Mortgages often have a 15-day grace period. If your payment is due on the 1st, you might not face a late fee until the 16th. But interest still accrues during this time.

Credit cards typically don't offer grace periods for payments. The due date is firm. However, they offer grace periods for interest on new purchases if you pay the full balance.

What Happens If You Miss the Due Date

Missing a due date triggers late fees. For credit cards, this is usually $30 to $41. For other bills, fees vary but can be substantial.

Your account might also be reported to credit bureaus if you're 30 days or more late. This can significantly hurt your credit score and stay on your report for seven years.

Some companies raise your interest rate if you miss payments. Credit cards may apply a penalty APR, which can be 29.99% or higher and last for months or indefinitely.

Changing Your Due Date

Many credit card companies let you request a different due date. This helps you align payments with your paychecks or other bills.

If you get paid on the 1st and your credit card is due on the 28th, you might request a change to the 5th. Now your due date comes right after payday, making it easier to pay on time.

To change your due date, call customer service or check your online account settings. Some companies let you make this change yourself online.

Strategic Due Date Planning

Some people stagger their due dates throughout the month to spread out expenses. Instead of all bills coming due on the 1st, they might have bills due on the 5th, 15th, and 25th.

This strategy can help with cash flow management, especially if you're paid twice monthly. You can align different bills with different paychecks.

Due Dates and Payment Processing

Understanding payment processing time is crucial. Online payments from your bank account usually process within 1-2 business days. Weekends and holidays don't count as business days.

If your due date falls on a weekend or holiday, check if the company moves the due date to the next business day. Not all companies do this, so you might need to pay on the Friday before.

Paying several days early ensures your payment arrives on time, even if there are processing delays or unexpected issues.

Automatic Payments and Due Dates

Setting up automatic payments removes the worry about missing due dates. The company withdraws the payment from your account automatically, usually on or a few days before the due date.

You can typically choose to pay the minimum payment, the full balance, or a fixed amount. Make sure you have enough money in your account on the due date to avoid overdraft fees.

Multiple Bills and Due Dates

Most people have multiple bills with different due dates. Credit cards, loans, utilities, insurance, rent, and subscriptions all have their own schedules.

Creating a bill calendar helps track all your due dates. You can use a physical calendar, digital calendar, or budgeting app to see what's due when.

Some people pay all bills on a single day each month, regardless of due date. This simplifies money management, though you might pay some bills earlier than necessary.

Key Takeaways

  • A due date is the deadline for making a payment without being late
  • It's when the company must receive your payment, not when you send it
  • Credit cards typically give you 21-25 days after the statement closes
  • Missing a due date results in late fees, usually $30-$41 for credit cards
  • Payments 30+ days late can be reported to credit bureaus
  • Many companies let you change your due date to align with your paychecks
  • Automatic payments ensure you never miss a due date
