What Does APR Mean?

5 min read

APR stands for Annual Percentage Rate. It's the yearly cost of borrowing money, shown as a percentage. When you borrow money through a credit card, loan, or mortgage, the APR tells you how much that borrowing will cost you each year.

Think of APR as the price tag for borrowing money. Just like knowing the price of groceries helps you budget, knowing the APR helps you understand what a loan will actually cost you.

How APR Works

When you borrow money, you pay back more than you borrowed. The extra amount is the cost of borrowing. APR shows that cost as a yearly percentage.

Here's a simple example. If you borrow $1,000 with a 10% APR, you'll pay about $100 in interest over one year. That means you'd owe around $1,100 total.

What APR Includes

APR typically includes two main costs. First, there's the interest rate, which is the basic cost of borrowing. Second, there are certain fees like loan origination fees or processing charges.

This is why APR is often higher than the interest rate alone. A loan might have a 9% interest rate but a 9.5% APR because of additional fees.

Real-World APR Examples

Credit cards commonly have APRs between 15% and 25%. If you carry a $1,000 balance on a card with 20% APR, you'd pay about $200 in interest over a year if you only made minimum payments.

Personal loans usually have APRs between 6% and 36%, depending on your credit. A $5,000 loan at 12% APR would cost you about $600 in interest over one year.

Mortgages typically have lower APRs, often between 3% and 8%. On a $200,000 mortgage at 6% APR, you'd pay about $12,000 in interest in the first year.

Why APR Matters

APR helps you compare different loans or credit cards fairly. Two credit cards might look similar, but one with 18% APR costs less than one with 24% APR.

The difference adds up quickly. On a $2,000 balance, an 18% APR costs about $360 per year in interest, while 24% APR costs about $480. That's $120 more for the same amount borrowed.

Types of APR

Credit cards often have different APRs for different uses. Purchase APR applies to things you buy. Cash advance APR applies when you withdraw cash, and it's usually higher.

Some credit cards offer promotional 0% APR for a limited time, like 12 or 18 months. After the promotional period ends, a regular APR applies to any remaining balance.

Fixed vs Variable APR

Fixed APR stays the same unless you're notified of a change. Variable APR can change based on market conditions, usually tied to something called the prime rate.

Most credit cards have variable APRs. If the prime rate goes up, your APR goes up too. This means your borrowing costs can change over time.

How to Find APR Information

Credit card companies must tell you the APR before you sign up. It's listed in the card's terms and conditions. You'll also find it on your monthly statement.

For loans, lenders provide APR information in the loan agreement. Federal law requires this disclosure so you can make informed decisions.

APR vs Interest Rate

While related, APR and interest rate aren't identical. The interest rate is just the cost of borrowing. APR includes the interest rate plus other fees.

For example, a mortgage might have a 5% interest rate but a 5.3% APR after including closing costs and fees. Always look at APR for the complete picture.

Key Takeaways

  • APR is the yearly cost of borrowing money, shown as a percentage
  • It includes interest plus certain fees, making it higher than the interest rate alone
  • Lower APR means lower borrowing costs
  • APR helps you compare different credit cards and loans fairly
  • Credit cards typically show APR between 15% and 25%
  • Variable APRs can change over time; fixed APRs stay the same
