APR vs APY Explained

Money Terms 5 min read

APR and APY sound similar but mean different things. APR applies to borrowing money (loans), while APY applies to saving money (accounts). Understanding both helps you make better financial decisions.

APR (Annual Percentage Rate): The yearly cost of borrowing money, including interest and fees. Used for loans, credit cards, and mortgages.

APY (Annual Percentage Yield): The yearly rate of return on savings or investments, including compound interest. Used for savings accounts, CDs, and investment accounts.

The Core Difference

Aspect APR (Borrowing) APY (Saving)
Applies to Loans, credit cards, mortgages Savings accounts, CDs, investments
What it means Cost to borrow Earnings from saving
Includes compound No (simple annual rate) Yes (compound interest)
Direction of money You pay out You earn
Goal Lower is better Higher is better

APR: When You Borrow

APR measures what borrowing costs you annually.

Example - Credit card with 18% APR:

  • Balance: $5,000
  • APR: 18%
  • Annual interest cost: ~$900
  • Monthly interest: ~$75

What APR includes:

  • Interest charges
  • Loan origination fees
  • Closing costs (mortgages)
  • Service fees

What APR doesn't include:

  • Late payment fees
  • Over-limit fees
  • Annual fees (credit cards)

APY: When You Save

APY measures what saving earns you annually, including compound interest.

Example - Savings account with 4% APY:

  • Deposit: $5,000
  • APY: 4%
  • Annual earnings: $204 (slightly more than 4% due to compounding)
  • Balance after 1 year: $5,204

Why APY is higher than stated interest rate:

  • Account shows "4% interest"
  • But interest compounds monthly
  • You earn interest on previously earned interest
  • APY captures this compound effect

Compound Interest Impact

Why APY > interest rate (for savings):

$10,000 in savings at "4% interest" compounded monthly:

  • Month 1: $10,000 × (4%/12) = $33.33 earned → Balance: $10,033.33
  • Month 2: $10,033.33 × (4%/12) = $33.44 earned → Balance: $10,066.77
  • ...continuing...
  • Month 12: Balance = $10,407.42

Simple calculation: $10,000 × 4% = $400

Actual earnings (APY): $407.42

APY: 4.07% (vs. 4% stated rate)

This is why banks advertise APY for savings - it looks better!

Side-by-Side Example

Scenario: You have $10,000

Option 1: Credit card debt at 18% APR

  • Direction: You owe $10,000
  • Cost: -$1,800/year in interest
  • After 1 year: -$11,800 owed (if no payments)

Option 2: Savings account at 4% APY

  • Direction: You have $10,000 saved
  • Earnings: +$407/year
  • After 1 year: $10,407 balance

Net difference: $2,207 per year

This demonstrates why carrying debt while having savings usually doesn't make sense - you lose far more in APR than you gain in APY.

Common APY Rates (2025)

Account Type Typical APY Earnings on $10,000
Regular savings (bank) 0.01% - 0.5% $1 - $50/year
High-yield savings 4.0% - 5.0% $400 - $500/year
Money market account 3.5% - 4.5% $350 - $450/year
1-year CD 4.5% - 5.5% $450 - $550/year

Which Number to Use

When comparing loans → Use APR

  • Shows total cost including fees
  • Standardized for comparison
  • Lower APR = less expensive loan

When comparing savings → Use APY

  • Shows actual earnings including compounding
  • True return on deposits
  • Higher APY = more money earned

Simple rule: For money you owe, focus on APR (lower is better). For money you save, focus on APY (higher is better).
