What Is Net Income?

6 min read Finance Basics

Net income is the money you actually receive after taxes and other deductions are taken out. It's often called "take-home pay" because it's what you take home from your paycheck.

Understanding net income is essential for budgeting. This is the real amount you have available to spend, save, and use for bills.

Net Income vs. Gross Income

These two terms are often confused, but they mean very different things.

Gross Income

Gross income is your total earnings before anything is deducted. When a job offer says "$50,000 per year," that's gross income.

If you work 40 hours at $25 per hour, your gross weekly income is $1,000. That's the amount before taxes and deductions.

Net Income

Net income is what remains after deductions. It's your gross income minus taxes, insurance, retirement contributions, and any other deductions.

From that $1,000 gross weekly income, you might receive $750 in your bank account. That $750 is your net income—your actual take-home pay.

Simple Comparison

Gross Income: What you earned

Net Income: What you received

The Difference: Taxes and deductions

What Gets Deducted?

Several things are typically taken from your gross income before you receive your paycheck.

Federal Income Tax

The federal government requires tax withholding from most paychecks. The amount depends on your income level and tax filing status.

Someone earning $50,000 might have about $5,000-$7,000 withheld annually for federal income tax, though this varies significantly.

State and Local Taxes

Most states (not all) have state income tax. Some cities and counties also have local income taxes.

State tax rates vary widely. Some states like Texas and Florida have no state income tax. Others like California have rates up to 13% on higher incomes.

Social Security and Medicare (FICA)

These are mandatory federal payroll taxes. Social Security takes 6.2% of your gross income (up to a limit). Medicare takes 1.45%.

On $4,000 gross monthly income, you'd pay $248 for Social Security and $58 for Medicare, totaling $306 in FICA taxes.

Health Insurance Premiums

If you have employer-provided health insurance, your portion of the premium is usually deducted from your paycheck.

This might be $100, $200, or more per month depending on your plan and coverage level.

Retirement Contributions

If you contribute to a 401(k) or similar retirement plan through work, that amount comes out before you receive your pay.

Contributing 5% of a $5,000 monthly gross income means $250 goes to retirement, reducing your take-home pay by that amount.

Other Possible Deductions

Additional deductions might include:

  • Dental and vision insurance
  • Life insurance
  • Flexible Spending Account (FSA) contributions
  • Health Savings Account (HSA) contributions
  • Union dues
  • Garnishments (if applicable)

Calculating Net Income: Example

Here's how gross income becomes net income in a realistic scenario.

Monthly Paycheck Breakdown

Gross Monthly Income: $5,000

Deductions:

  • Federal income tax: $625
  • State income tax: $200
  • Social Security (6.2%): $310
  • Medicare (1.45%): $73
  • Health insurance: $180
  • 401(k) contribution (6%): $300
  • Dental insurance: $25

Total Deductions: $1,713

Net Monthly Income: $3,287

This person's gross income is $5,000, but their net income—what actually hits their bank account—is $3,287. That's about 66% of gross income.

Why Net Income Matters for Budgeting

When creating a budget, always use net income, not gross income. Your net income is what you have available to spend.

Someone earning $60,000 gross annually might think they have $5,000 per month to work with. But if their net income is only $3,800 per month, budgeting for $5,000 will create serious problems.

Common Budgeting Mistake

Many beginners budget using gross income and wonder why they always run short on money. The issue is they're planning to spend money they never actually receive.

Always check your actual paycheck amount—your net income—and budget from that number.

Finding Your Net Income

Your pay stub shows both gross and net income clearly. Look for these terms:

  • Gross Pay: Total earnings before deductions
  • Deductions: Everything taken out
  • Net Pay: Final amount deposited to your account

The net pay amount matches what appears in your bank account on payday.

Annual vs. Monthly Net Income

You can calculate net income for any time period.

Annual Net Income

To find annual net income, add up all the net pay from your paychecks for the year. Or multiply your typical monthly net income by 12.

If you receive $3,200 net per month, your annual net income is approximately $38,400.

Monthly Net Income

For monthly budgeting, use your monthly net income. If you're paid biweekly, you'll receive two paychecks most months and three paychecks twice a year.

Many people budget based on receiving two paychecks per month and treat the third paycheck (when it occurs) as extra for savings or irregular expenses.

Increasing Net Income

There are two main ways to increase your net income: earn more or reduce deductions.

Earning More

Getting a raise, switching to a higher-paying job, or adding income sources increases gross income, which typically increases net income too (though higher earnings mean higher taxes).

Reducing Deductions

Some deductions are mandatory (taxes, FICA), but others are optional or adjustable:

  • Reduce retirement contributions (though this affects long-term savings)
  • Choose a health insurance plan with lower premiums
  • Adjust tax withholding if you consistently get large refunds
  • Drop optional insurance if not needed

Be careful reducing beneficial deductions like retirement savings. The goal is to optimize, not just maximize take-home pay at the expense of future security.

Net Income for Different Income Types

Self-Employment

If you're self-employed, you receive the gross amount from clients. You're responsible for setting aside money for taxes yourself.

A freelancer who earns $5,000 in a month receives the full $5,000. But they need to save approximately 25-30% for taxes, leaving about $3,500-$3,750 as true net income.

Multiple Jobs

If you have multiple income sources, calculate net income for each separately, then add them together.

Example: Multiple Income Sources

Main job net income: $2,800/month

Weekend job net income: $450/month

Total net income: $3,250/month

Key Takeaways

  • Net income is what you actually receive after deductions; gross income is before deductions
  • Common deductions include federal and state taxes, FICA, insurance, and retirement contributions
  • Always budget using net income, not gross income
  • Your pay stub shows both gross and net income clearly
  • Net income is typically 60-75% of gross income for most workers
  • Check your actual paycheck amount to know your true net income
