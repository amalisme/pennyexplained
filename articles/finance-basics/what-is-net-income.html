What is Net Income?

Finance Basics 5 min read

Net income is what you actually receive in your bank account after all deductions. Understanding net income is crucial for realistic budgeting.

Advertisement - Leaderboard (728x90)

Net income is your take-home pay - the actual amount of money deposited into your account. It's what you have available to spend, save, and pay bills. Understanding net income is one of the fundamental personal finance basics.

Gross Income vs Net Income

Gross Income

Gross income is what you earn before any deductions. Learn more about different types of income and how they work:

  • The salary amount in your job offer
  • What you see as "annual salary"
  • Your hourly rate times hours worked
  • The number before taxes and deductions

Example: Job offer of $60,000 per year = $5,000 per month gross

Net Income

Net income is what's left after all deductions:

  • Gross income minus federal taxes
  • Minus state and local taxes
  • Minus Social Security (6.2%)
  • Minus Medicare (1.45%)
  • Minus health insurance premiums
  • Minus 401(k) contributions
  • Minus other deductions

Same example: $60,000 salary might result in $3,600-3,900 monthly net income

What Gets Deducted?

Mandatory Deductions

Federal Income Tax: Varies by income level and filing status (10% to 37% on different portions of income)

State Income Tax: Varies by state (0% to 13%+). Seven states have no income tax.

FICA Taxes:

  • Social Security: 6.2% of gross (up to wage limit)
  • Medicare: 1.45% of all gross income

Voluntary Deductions

Health Insurance: Your share of premium (employer often pays part)

Retirement Contributions: 401(k), 403(b), or similar plan

Other Benefits:

  • Dental and vision insurance
  • Life insurance
  • Disability insurance
  • Health Savings Account (HSA)
  • Flexible Spending Account (FSA)

Calculating Net Income

Example paycheck breakdown:

Gross monthly income: $5,000

Deductions:

  • Federal tax: -$600
  • State tax: -$250
  • Social Security: -$310 (6.2%)
  • Medicare: -$73 (1.45%)
  • Health insurance: -$150
  • 401(k): -$250 (5%)

Net monthly income: $3,367

This is what hits your bank account and what you can actually use for budgeting.

Why Net Income Matters

Realistic budgeting: If you budget based on gross income, you'll consistently overspend because you don't have access to that full amount.

Example of the problem:

  • Gross income: $5,000
  • Budget rent at 30% of gross: $1,500
  • But net income is only $3,367
  • $1,500 rent is actually 45% of take-home
  • Leaves only $1,867 for everything else

Correct approach: Budget based on the $3,367 you actually receive.

The 30% Rule Example

Housing guidelines often say "spend no more than 30% on housing." But 30% of what?

Wrong way (using gross):

  • $60,000 salary รท 12 = $5,000/month gross
  • 30% = $1,500 for housing

Right way (using net):

  • $60,000 salary = ~$3,600/month net
  • 30% = $1,080 for housing

Using gross income overstates what you can afford by about $400/month in this example.

Increasing Net Income

You can increase net income without changing gross by:

Adjust tax withholding: If you get big refunds, you're withholding too much. Adjust W-4 to keep more per paycheck (but don't owe at tax time).

Review benefits: If you have multiple insurance options, choosing a cheaper plan increases take-home (but ensure adequate coverage).

Reduce pre-tax deductions: Temporarily lowering 401(k) contributions increases net income (but impacts retirement savings).

Note: Some of these tradeoffs aren't worth it. Don't sacrifice retirement or necessary insurance just for more monthly cash.

Self-Employment Net Income

If you're self-employed, calculating net income is different:

Gross revenue: All money received from business

Minus business expenses: Costs to run the business

= Net business income

Minus taxes (need to save ~30%):

= Actual take-home

Example:

  • Earn $6,000 from freelancing
  • Business expenses: $1,000
  • Net business income: $5,000
  • Set aside for taxes: $1,500 (30%)
  • Actual take-home: $3,500

Understanding your true net income helps you maintain positive cash flow and avoid financial stress.
