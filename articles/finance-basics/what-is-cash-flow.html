What Is Cash Flow?

6 min read Finance Basics

Cash flow is the movement of money in and out of your accounts. Money coming in (like your paycheck) is cash inflow. Money going out (like paying bills) is cash outflow.

Understanding your cash flow helps you see whether you're gaining money, losing money, or breaking even each month.

How Cash Flow Works

Think of cash flow like water flowing through a pipe. Money flows into your checking account from various sources. Money flows out when you spend or pay bills.

The difference between what flows in and what flows out determines your cash flow situation.

Cash Inflow (Money Coming In)

Cash inflow includes any money you receive:

  • Paycheck from your job
  • Freelance or side gig payments
  • Tax refunds
  • Government benefits
  • Gifts or bonuses
  • Money transferred from savings
  • Refunds or reimbursements

If you receive a $3,000 paycheck, that's $3,000 of cash inflow for the month.

Cash Outflow (Money Going Out)

Cash outflow is any money you spend or transfer out:

  • Rent or mortgage payment
  • Utility bills
  • Groceries and gas
  • Credit card payments
  • Loan payments
  • Money transferred to savings
  • Subscriptions and services
  • Any other purchases

If you pay $1,200 rent, $150 utilities, $400 groceries, and $800 in other expenses, that's $2,550 of cash outflow.

Positive vs. Negative Cash Flow

Your cash flow is either positive, negative, or neutral depending on the relationship between inflow and outflow.

Positive Cash Flow

Positive cash flow means more money comes in than goes out. You're gaining money each period.

Example: Positive Cash Flow

Monthly Cash Inflow: $3,800

Monthly Cash Outflow: $3,200

Cash Flow: +$600 (positive)

This person has $600 left over at the end of the month. They can save it, invest it, or spend it on extra things.

Negative Cash Flow

Negative cash flow means more money goes out than comes in. You're losing money each period.

Example: Negative Cash Flow

Monthly Cash Inflow: $3,200

Monthly Cash Outflow: $3,600

Cash Flow: -$400 (negative)

This person is short $400 each month. They need to either reduce spending, increase income, or use savings/credit to cover the gap.

Neutral Cash Flow

Neutral cash flow means inflow and outflow are equal. You break evenâ€”nothing gained, nothing lost.

Example: Neutral Cash Flow

Monthly Cash Inflow: $3,500

Monthly Cash Outflow: $3,500

Cash Flow: $0 (neutral)

This person uses every dollar they earn. They're not saving or going into debt, just maintaining the status quo.

Why Cash Flow Matters

Monitoring cash flow helps you understand your financial health at a glance. It answers the critical question: "Am I gaining or losing ground financially?"

Avoiding Financial Trouble

Persistent negative cash flow leads to problems. If you consistently spend more than you earn, you'll eventually run out of savings or accumulate debt.

Catching negative cash flow early lets you make adjustments before serious issues develop.

Building Wealth

Positive cash flow is essential for building wealth. The money left over each month can go toward savings, investments, or paying down debt.

Someone with $500 positive cash flow each month has $6,000 per year to build wealth. Over time, this compounds significantly.

Financial Flexibility

Strong positive cash flow gives you options. You can handle unexpected expenses, save for goals, or make strategic financial moves.

Calculating Your Cash Flow

To calculate cash flow, subtract total outflow from total inflow for a specific period.

Monthly Cash Flow Calculation

Example: Sarah's Monthly Cash Flow

Cash Inflow:

  • Salary (net): $3,400
  • Freelance work: $300

Total Inflow: $3,700

Cash Outflow:

  • Rent: $1,200
  • Utilities: $140
  • Groceries: $380
  • Car payment: $280
  • Insurance: $160
  • Gas: $100
  • Phone: $75
  • Eating out: $180
  • Entertainment: $120
  • Misc: $100

Total Outflow: $2,735

Cash Flow: $3,700 - $2,735 = +$965

Sarah has positive monthly cash flow of $965. This is money she can save, invest, or use for other financial goals.

Improving Cash Flow

If your cash flow is negative or you want to increase positive cash flow, you have two main options: increase inflow or decrease outflow.

Increasing Cash Inflow

Ways to bring more money in:

  • Ask for a raise at work
  • Take on extra hours or overtime
  • Start a side gig or freelance work
  • Sell items you no longer need
  • Find a higher-paying job

Adding even $200-$300 per month in extra income can shift negative cash flow to positive or boost your savings rate significantly.

Decreasing Cash Outflow

Ways to reduce money going out:

  • Cut unnecessary subscriptions
  • Reduce eating out frequency
  • Find cheaper insurance rates
  • Lower utility usage
  • Use grocery lists to avoid impulse purchases
  • Refinance loans for lower payments
  • Move to cheaper housing (if feasible)

Finding $300 in monthly spending to cut has the same effect on cash flow as earning an extra $300.

Cash Flow vs. Income

Cash flow and income are related but different concepts.

Income is just the money coming in. Cash flow considers both income and spending to show your net position.

Someone earning $100,000 per year (high income) could have negative cash flow if they spend $110,000. Someone earning $40,000 (lower income) could have positive cash flow if they spend only $35,000.

Tracking Cash Flow Over Time

Monitoring cash flow month to month reveals patterns and trends.

Monthly Tracking

Check your cash flow at the end of each month. Are you consistently positive? Is the amount growing? Are there problem months?

If you're positive most months but negative in December due to holiday spending, you can plan for that by saving extra in other months.

Spotting Trends

Three months of declining cash flow signals a developing problem. Six months of increasing positive cash flow shows improving financial health.

Example: 6-Month Cash Flow Trend

  • January: +$450
  • February: +$520
  • March: +$380
  • April: +$600
  • May: +$490
  • June: +$550

Average: +$498/month

Trend: Positive and stable

Common Cash Flow Challenges

Irregular Income

Freelancers and commission workers have fluctuating cash inflow. Some months are high, others low.

Solution: Budget based on your lowest typical month. Save excess from high months to cover low months.

Large Irregular Expenses

Annual insurance premiums, property taxes, or irregular car repairs can create negative cash flow in specific months.

Solution: Set aside money monthly for these known expenses so they don't disrupt cash flow when due.

Seasonal Variations

Utility bills spike in summer/winter. Holiday spending increases in November/December.

Solution: Expect these variations and plan for higher outflow in certain months.

Key Takeaways

  • Cash flow is the difference between money coming in and money going out
  • Positive cash flow means you're gaining money; negative means you're losing money
  • Calculate cash flow by subtracting total outflow from total inflow
  • Consistent positive cash flow is essential for financial health and building wealth
  • Improve cash flow by increasing income or decreasing expenses
  • Track cash flow monthly to spot trends and address problems early
  • Plan for irregular expenses and income to maintain stable cash flow
