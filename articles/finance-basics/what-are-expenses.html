What Are Expenses?

Finance Basics 6 min read

Expenses are all the things you spend money on. Understanding and tracking your expenses is essential for managing your money effectively.

Advertisement - Leaderboard (728x90)

Expenses are simply what you spend money on - everything from rent and groceries to entertainment and clothing. Every time money leaves your account or pocket, that's an expense.

Fixed Expenses

Fixed expenses stay the same (or nearly the same) every month. They're predictable and usually mandatory.

Common fixed expenses:

  • Rent or mortgage: Housing payment stays the same monthly
  • Car payment: Fixed amount until loan is paid off
  • Insurance: Car, health, life insurance premiums
  • Loan payments: Student loans, personal loans
  • Subscriptions: Netflix, Spotify, gym membership
  • Phone bill: Usually consistent monthly

Example monthly fixed expenses:

  • Rent: $1,200
  • Car payment: $350
  • Car insurance: $150
  • Phone: $60
  • Gym: $40
  • Total fixed: $1,800

Variable Expenses

Variable expenses change from month to month. The amount varies based on usage or choices.

Common variable expenses:

  • Groceries: Changes based on what you buy
  • Gas: Varies with driving and prices
  • Utilities: Electricity, water usage changes seasonally
  • Dining out: Depends on how often you eat out
  • Entertainment: Movies, concerts, activities
  • Clothing: Not every month, varies in amount
  • Personal care: Haircuts, toiletries

Example monthly variable expenses:

  • Groceries: $350-450
  • Gas: $120-180
  • Utilities: $100-200
  • Dining out: $150-300
  • Entertainment: $50-150

Understanding the difference between fixed and variable expenses helps you identify where you have flexibility in your budget.

Discretionary vs Non-Discretionary

Non-Discretionary (Needs)

Essential expenses you can't easily eliminate:

  • Housing (rent/mortgage)
  • Basic food (groceries)
  • Utilities
  • Transportation to work
  • Minimum debt payments
  • Health insurance

Discretionary (Wants)

Optional expenses that enhance your life but aren't essential:

  • Dining out
  • Entertainment
  • Cable TV/streaming
  • Hobbies
  • Vacation
  • New clothes (beyond basics)

Why the distinction matters: When cutting expenses, discretionary items are the first to reduce. You can survive without Netflix, but not without housing or food.

Periodic Expenses

These don't happen every month but need planning:

Quarterly expenses:

  • Car insurance (if paid quarterly)
  • Certain subscriptions

Annual expenses:

  • Car registration
  • Amazon Prime membership
  • Property taxes (if not in mortgage)
  • Annual subscriptions

Irregular expenses:

  • Car maintenance/repairs
  • Medical bills
  • Gifts (birthdays, holidays)
  • Home repairs

Budget strategy: Calculate yearly total, divide by 12, save that amount monthly.

Example: Car insurance costs $600/year. Save $50/month so you have the money when the bill comes.

Why Tracking Expenses Matters

Shows where money goes: Most people underestimate spending. Tracking reveals the truth about your habits.

Identifies waste: You might discover you're spending $200/month on takeout coffee without realizing it.

Enables budgeting: You can't create a realistic budget without knowing your actual expenses.

Helps reach goals: Finding $200/month in unnecessary spending means $200/month toward savings or debt payoff. This directly improves your cash flow and financial flexibility.

How to Track Expenses

Simple methods:

  • Save all receipts for a month
  • Write purchases in a notebook
  • Use a basic spreadsheet
  • Check bank/credit card statements
  • Use a budgeting app

Track for at least one month to see your real spending patterns. Many people are shocked by what they discover. Our guide on tracking expenses manually provides more detailed methods.

Average Expense Breakdown

Here's a typical expense allocation for a $3,500 monthly budget:

  • Housing: 30% ($1,050)
  • Transportation: 15% ($525)
  • Food: 12% ($420)
  • Utilities: 5% ($175)
  • Insurance: 10% ($350)
  • Savings: 15% ($525)
  • Personal/Entertainment: 8% ($280)
  • Debt payments: 5% ($175)

Your percentages will vary based on cost of living, income level, and personal choices. Use this as a rough guide, not a strict rule.
