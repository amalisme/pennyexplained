What is Income?

Finance Basics 5 min read

Income is money you receive. Understanding the different types of income and how to calculate what you actually have to spend helps you budget effectively.

Advertisement - Leaderboard (728x90)

Income is any money that comes to you. For most people, this mainly means paychecks from work, but income can come from many sources.

Types of Income

Earned Income

Money you receive in exchange for work or services:

  • Wages: Hourly pay from a job
  • Salary: Fixed yearly pay divided into regular paychecks
  • Tips: Extra money received for service
  • Commissions: Pay based on sales or performance
  • Self-employment income: Money earned from your own business

Example: You work 40 hours per week at $20/hour = $800/week = $3,467/month before taxes.

Unearned Income

Money that doesn't come from active work:

  • Interest: Money your bank pays you for keeping money in savings
  • Dividends: Share of profits from stocks you own
  • Rental income: Money from renting out property
  • Capital gains: Profit from selling investments
  • Royalties: Payments for use of your creative work

Gross Income vs Net Income

This is crucial to understand for budgeting:

Gross Income

The total amount you earn before any deductions:

  • What your employer promises to pay you
  • The amount on your job offer letter
  • What you see as your "salary" or "annual income"

Example: Salary of $60,000 per year = $5,000 per month gross income

Net Income (Take-Home Pay)

What actually goes into your bank account after deductions:

  • Gross income minus federal taxes
  • Minus state/local taxes
  • Minus Social Security and Medicare
  • Minus health insurance premiums
  • Minus 401(k) contributions
  • Minus other deductions

Same example: $60,000 salary might result in only $3,800 monthly take-home pay after all deductions.

Critical rule: Always budget based on net income (what you actually receive), not gross income.

Calculating Your Monthly Income

Different pay schedules require different calculations:

Weekly Paychecks

Multiply weekly amount by 52, then divide by 12:

($800 per week × 52 weeks) ÷ 12 months = $3,467/month

Bi-Weekly Paychecks (Every 2 Weeks)

Multiply by 26, then divide by 12:

($1,600 every 2 weeks × 26) ÷ 12 months = $3,467/month

Semi-Monthly Paychecks (Twice a Month)

Multiply by 2:

$1,800 twice monthly × 2 = $3,600/month

Monthly Paychecks

Already monthly - use that number directly.

Variable Income

If your income changes monthly (commissions, tips, freelance), calculating average income helps with budgeting:

Method: Add up last 3-6 months of income, then divide by number of months.

Example:

  • January: $3,200
  • February: $2,800
  • March: $3,500
  • April: $2,900
  • May: $3,400
  • June: $3,100

Total: $18,900 ÷ 6 months = $3,150 average monthly income

Budget based on this average, and save extra from high-income months to cover low-income months.

Multiple Income Sources

Many people have income from several sources:

Example:

  • Primary job: $3,000/month
  • Side hustle: $500/month
  • Investment dividends: $50/month
  • Total monthly income: $3,550

Add all sources together to know your total income available for budgeting.

Why Understanding Income Matters

Accurate budgeting: You can't budget effectively if you don't know exactly how much money you have coming in.

Tax planning: Different income types are taxed differently. Understanding this helps you plan for tax season.

Financial decisions: Knowing your income helps you decide how much house you can afford, whether you can take on a car payment, or how much to save.

Goal setting: You can set realistic financial goals when you know exactly how much income you have to work with.
