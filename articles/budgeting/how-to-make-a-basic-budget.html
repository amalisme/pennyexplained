How to Make a Basic Budget

Budgeting 8 min read

Creating your first budget is easier than you think. Follow these simple steps to take control of your money.

Advertisement - Leaderboard (728x90)

Making a budget doesn't require complicated software or advanced math skills. You just need to know how much money comes in and where it goes out. That's it - everything else is details.

This guide walks you through creating your first budget from scratch. By the end, you'll have a working budget you can use immediately.

What You'll Need

Before you start, gather these things:

  • Your last 2-3 bank statements or credit card statements
  • Your most recent pay stub
  • A list of upcoming bills
  • Something to write with (paper, spreadsheet, or app)

Having this information ready makes the process much faster and more accurate than guessing.

Step 1: Calculate Your Monthly Income

Start by figuring out how much money you receive each month. Use your net income (take-home pay after taxes), not your gross salary. The money that actually hits your bank account is what you have to budget.

What to include:

  • Paycheck after taxes and deductions
  • Side hustle or freelance income
  • Regular bonuses or commissions
  • Any other consistent income

What NOT to include:

  • One-time windfalls (tax refunds, gifts)
  • Irregular or unpredictable income
  • Money you might get someday

Example 1: You're paid $2,500 twice a month (bi-weekly). Your monthly income is $2,500 × 2 = $5,000.

Example 2: Your main job pays $3,200 monthly, and you freelance for $300-500 monthly. Use the lower amount to be safe: $3,200 + $300 = $3,500 monthly income.

If You Have Variable Income

For freelancers, commission-based workers, or anyone with fluctuating income, look at the past 6-12 months. Find your lowest monthly income and use that number. When you earn more, the extra goes to savings or one-time expenses.

Example: Your monthly income over 6 months was $4,500, $3,200, $5,800, $4,100, $3,800, and $6,000. Your lowest was $3,200, so budget using $3,200. This prevents overspending in low-income months.

Step 2: List All Your Expenses

Write down everything you spend money on in a typical month. Look at your bank and credit card statements from the past 2-3 months to help remember everything.

Fixed Expenses (Same Every Month)

These amounts don't change month to month, making them easy to predict:

  • Rent or mortgage payment
  • Car payment or lease
  • Insurance (car, health, home, life)
  • Loan payments (student loans, personal loans)
  • Subscriptions (Netflix, Spotify, gym membership)
  • Phone and internet bills

Example fixed expenses: Rent ($1,400), car payment ($350), car insurance ($120), student loan ($200), gym ($40), phone ($50), streaming services ($30). Total: $2,190 in fixed monthly expenses.

Variable Expenses (Change Each Month)

These amounts fluctuate based on usage and choices:

  • Groceries and household supplies
  • Gas and transportation
  • Utilities (electricity, water, gas)
  • Dining out and takeout
  • Entertainment and hobbies
  • Clothing and personal care
  • Pet expenses

For variable expenses, look at your average spending over the past few months and round up slightly to be safe.

Example variable expenses: Groceries ($450), utilities ($150), gas ($120), dining out ($200), entertainment ($100), personal care ($60). Total: $1,080 in variable monthly expenses.

Don't Forget Irregular Expenses

These hit annually or quarterly but people often forget them, causing budget disasters:

  • Car registration and inspections
  • Annual insurance premiums
  • Holiday and birthday gifts
  • Annual subscriptions (Amazon Prime, Costco membership)
  • Property taxes (if you own)
  • Seasonal expenses (back to school, winter heating)

How to handle them: Add up all yearly irregular expenses, divide by 12, and save that amount monthly.

Example: Car registration ($120/year), gifts ($600/year), Amazon Prime ($139/year), car maintenance ($480/year) = $1,339 total. Divide by 12 = $112 to save monthly for irregular expenses.

Step 3: Categorize Your Spending

Group your expenses into broad categories. This helps you see patterns and identify where most of your money goes. Start with 6-8 main categories - don't overcomplicate it.

Basic categories to use:

  • Housing: Rent/mortgage, utilities, maintenance, insurance
  • Transportation: Car payment, gas, insurance, maintenance, public transit
  • Food: Groceries, dining out, coffee, work lunches
  • Personal: Clothing, haircuts, phone, subscriptions, personal care
  • Health: Insurance, medications, doctor visits, gym
  • Entertainment: Hobbies, movies, events, vacations
  • Debt Payments: Credit cards, student loans, personal loans
  • Savings: Emergency fund, retirement, goals

Step 4: Set Spending Limits for Each Category

Now assign dollar amounts to each category based on your total income. The sum of all categories should equal your monthly income (or less if you want money left over).

Use the 50/30/20 rule as a starting framework: 50% needs, 30% wants, 20% savings. Adjust these percentages based on your situation.

Complete Budget Example

Monthly income: $5,000

Housing ($1,500 - 30%):

  • Rent: $1,200
  • Utilities: $200
  • Renter's insurance: $20
  • Internet: $80

Transportation ($700 - 14%):

  • Car payment: $350
  • Car insurance: $120
  • Gas: $150
  • Maintenance fund: $80

Food ($600 - 12%):

  • Groceries: $400
  • Dining out: $150
  • Coffee/snacks: $50

Personal ($350 - 7%):

  • Phone: $60
  • Subscriptions: $40
  • Clothing: $100
  • Personal care: $70
  • Gym: $80

Health ($250 - 5%):

  • Health insurance: $200
  • Medications/copays: $50

Entertainment ($300 - 6%):

  • Streaming services: $40
  • Hobbies: $100
  • Events/activities: $160

Debt Payments ($400 - 8%):

  • Student loan minimum: $250
  • Credit card: $150

Savings ($750 - 15%):

  • Emergency fund: $400
  • Retirement (401k): $250
  • Vacation fund: $100

Irregular Expenses ($150 - 3%):

  • Saved for annual costs: $150

Total Budget: $5,000

This budget accounts for every dollar and balances needs, wants, debt payoff, and savings.

Step 5: Track Your Actual Spending

Creating the budget is just the first step. Now you need to track your spending throughout the month to see if you're following the plan.

Tracking Methods

Option 1: Notebook or planner - Write down every expense as it happens. Simple and free.

Option 2: Spreadsheet - Use Excel or Google Sheets to list expenses and calculate totals automatically.

Option 3: Budgeting app - Apps like YNAB, Mint, or EveryDollar connect to your bank and categorize automatically.

Option 4: Budget calculator - Use a budget calculator to input income and expenses, and it does the math for you.

Pick whichever method you'll actually use consistently. The best tracking system is the one you stick with.

Check Progress Weekly

Don't wait until month's end to see how you're doing. Check in weekly:

  • Review how much you've spent in each category
  • See how much budget remains for the rest of the month
  • Slow down spending in categories nearing their limit
  • Adjust if needed (life happens!)

Example: It's the 15th. You've spent $250 of your $400 grocery budget. You have $150 left for the remaining 15 days, or about $10 per day. Knowing this helps you plan shopping trips.

Step 6: Review and Adjust Monthly

At the end of each month, review your budget performance. Compare what you planned to spend versus what you actually spent.

Questions to Ask

  • Which categories went over budget? Why?
  • Which categories had money left? Can you reduce them?
  • Were there unexpected expenses?
  • What worked well? What needs adjustment?

Make Adjustments

Your budget should reflect reality, not wishful thinking. If you consistently overspend groceries but underspend entertainment, shift money between those categories for next month.

Example adjustments:

  • Budgeted $300 for groceries, actually spent $450 → Increase to $425 next month and find $125 to cut elsewhere
  • Budgeted $200 for entertainment, only spent $120 → Reduce to $150 next month and reallocate $50
  • Unexpected $180 car repair → Add $50 monthly to maintenance fund going forward

Common First-Time Budget Challenges

Challenge 1: Underestimating Expenses

First-time budgeters often underestimate how much they really spend. After tracking for a month, they're shocked by the real numbers.

Solution: Be honest and realistic. Use actual past spending data, not optimistic guesses.

Challenge 2: Not Planning for Fun

Cutting all entertainment and fun money makes budgets unsustainable. You'll eventually rebel and overspend.

Solution: Include reasonable amounts for entertainment, dining out, and hobbies. Budgeting doesn't mean no fun.

Challenge 3: Giving Up After One Bad Month

Most people blow their first budget and quit. This is a mistake - budgeting is a skill that takes practice.

Solution: Expect the first 2-3 months to be rough. Each month gets easier as you learn and adjust.

Budget Success Tips

  • Start simple: Begin with broad categories, add detail only if needed
  • Round up expenses: Better to overestimate than underestimate
  • Build in a buffer: Include $100-200 "miscellaneous" for surprises
  • Pay yourself first: Move money to savings when you get paid, not at month's end
  • Review together: If you share finances, budget with your partner weekly
  • Celebrate wins: Acknowledge when you stay on budget or reach savings goals
  • Be flexible: Life changes, and your budget should too

Your Budget is Ready

Congratulations! You've just created your first budget. Now comes the important part: actually using it. Put it somewhere visible, check it regularly, and adjust as you learn.

Remember, a budget isn't about perfection or restriction. It's a tool that helps you spend money on what matters, avoid wasting it on what doesn't, and build the financial future you want.

Key Takeaways

  • Calculate monthly net income (take-home pay after taxes)
  • List all expenses: fixed, variable, and irregular
  • Group expenses into 6-8 broad categories
  • Set spending limits so total expenses equal income
  • Track actual spending weekly using your preferred method
  • Review and adjust monthly based on real spending patterns
  • Expect 2-3 months of learning before budgeting clicks
  • A good budget includes money for both needs and fun
