How to Make a Basic Budget

8 min read Budgeting

Making a basic budget takes about 30 minutes and requires just a pen, paper, and your recent bank statements. You'll list your income, list your expenses, assign dollar amounts to each category, and make sure the numbers balance.

This guide walks you through creating your first budget from scratch, even if you've never done it before.

What You'll Need

Before starting, gather these items:

  • Recent pay stubs showing your take-home pay
  • Bank statements from the last 2-3 months
  • Credit card statements (if you use credit cards)
  • List of recurring bills
  • Paper and pen, or a spreadsheet/budgeting app

You need this information to create accurate budget numbers based on reality, not guesses.

Step 1: Calculate Your Monthly Income

Start with your actual take-home pay—the amount deposited into your account after taxes and deductions.

If You're Paid Monthly

This is simple. If you receive $3,400 once per month, that's your monthly income.

If You're Paid Biweekly

You receive 26 paychecks per year (two per month, plus two extra). To find monthly income, multiply one paycheck by 2.

Example: Biweekly Pay

Paycheck amount: $1,450

Monthly income: $1,450 × 2 = $2,900

(Note: You'll receive three paychecks twice a year)

If You're Paid Weekly

Multiply your weekly paycheck by 4 to get an approximate monthly amount.

Weekly paycheck of $700 becomes $2,800 per month for budgeting purposes.

If You Have Irregular Income

Look at the last 6-12 months of income. Use the lowest month or calculate an average. It's better to budget conservatively than optimistically.

Multiple Income Sources

Add all sources together. If you earn $2,600 from your main job and $400 from weekend work, your total monthly income is $3,000.

Step 2: List Your Fixed Expenses

Fixed expenses are costs that stay the same every month. These are usually your largest, most predictable bills.

Common Fixed Expenses

Write down your actual amounts for:

  • Rent or mortgage payment
  • Car payment
  • Car insurance
  • Health insurance (if paid separately)
  • Student loan payments
  • Phone bill
  • Internet service
  • Subscriptions (streaming, gym, etc.)

Example: Maria's Fixed Expenses

  • Rent: $1,150
  • Car payment: $295
  • Car insurance: $140
  • Phone: $75
  • Internet: $60
  • Streaming services: $35
  • Gym: $40

Total Fixed: $1,795

Step 3: Estimate Your Variable Expenses

Variable expenses change from month to month. Look at your bank statements to see what you typically spend.

Common Variable Expenses

  • Groceries
  • Gas for your car
  • Electric and water bills
  • Eating out/restaurants
  • Entertainment
  • Clothing
  • Personal care (haircuts, toiletries)
  • Household items

How to Estimate

Review your last 2-3 months of spending. Find the average or use the highest month to be safe.

If you spent $380, $420, and $450 on groceries over three months, budget $450 or an average of $417. Starting higher prevents overshooting.

Example: Maria's Variable Expenses

  • Groceries: $420
  • Gas: $110
  • Electric: $85
  • Water: $35
  • Eating out: $180
  • Entertainment: $100
  • Personal care: $60
  • Miscellaneous: $80

Total Variable: $1,070

Step 4: Include Savings as an "Expense"

Treat savings like a bill you must pay. Add a line for savings in your budget.

Even if it's just $50 or $100 to start, putting savings in your budget ensures it happens. You can increase this amount as you find more room in your budget.

Example: Maria's Savings

Emergency fund: $200

Vacation fund: $100

Total Savings: $300

Step 5: Add Up Everything

Now add your fixed expenses, variable expenses, and savings together to get your total monthly spending.

Example: Maria's Total Budget

Fixed expenses: $1,795

Variable expenses: $1,070

Savings: $300

Total: $3,165

Step 6: Compare to Your Income

This is the critical step. Your income must be greater than or equal to your total expenses plus savings.

If Income Is Higher

Great! You have money left over. Decide what to do with it—add it to savings, pay extra on debt, or add a new budget category.

Example: Surplus Budget

Income: $3,400

Expenses + Savings: $3,165

Leftover: $235

Maria can add this to savings or create a new category.

If Expenses Are Higher

You need to make adjustments. Either cut expenses or find ways to increase income.

Example: Deficit Budget

Income: $2,900

Expenses + Savings: $3,165

Shortfall: -$265

This doesn't work. Maria needs to cut $265 from expenses.

Step 7: Adjust Until It Balances

If your budget doesn't balance, you need to make changes. Look for areas to cut.

Easier Cuts

Start with variable expenses and wants:

  • Reduce eating out from $180 to $100
  • Cut entertainment from $100 to $50
  • Lower grocery budget by buying store brands
  • Cancel unused subscriptions

Harder Cuts

If you need bigger changes, consider:

  • Getting a roommate to split rent
  • Switching to a cheaper phone plan
  • Shopping for lower car insurance rates
  • Temporarily reducing or pausing savings

The goal is to balance without making life miserable. Find sustainable cuts you can maintain long-term.

Put your final budget in writing. This can be on paper, in a spreadsheet, or in a budgeting app.

Step 9: Track Your Spending

Creating the budget is just the start. Now you need to track actual spending against your plan.

Each time you spend money, note which category it falls under. At least weekly, check how much you've spent in each category versus your budget.

If you're at $280 of your $420 grocery budget with two weeks left, you're on track. If you're at $380 with two weeks left, you need to slow down.

Step 10: Review and Adjust Monthly

At the end of each month, compare what you actually spent to what you budgeted. Use this information to improve next month's budget.

Maybe you consistently overspend on groceries by $50. Increase that budget category and decrease another to compensate.

Budgets evolve over time. Your first budget won't be perfect, and that's okay. Each month teaches you more about your spending patterns.

Tips for Budget Success

Start Simple

Don't create 30 detailed categories. Start with 8-10 broad categories and refine later if needed.

Be Realistic

Budget for reality, not fantasy. If you spend $400 on groceries, don't budget $250 hoping to magically change overnight.

Include Fun Money

Budget some money for entertainment or personal spending. A budget that's all sacrifice won't last.

Plan for Irregular Expenses

Annual expenses like car registration or holiday gifts should be divided by 12 and included monthly so you're ready when they come.

Use Round Numbers

Budget in round numbers when possible. $400 for groceries is easier to track than $387.43.

Common Beginner Mistakes

Avoid these pitfalls as you create your first budget:

  • Using gross income instead of net income
  • Forgetting irregular expenses like oil changes or gifts
  • Being too restrictive with no flexibility
  • Creating the budget but not tracking spending
  • Giving up after one imperfect month
  • Making it too complicated with too many categories

Key Takeaways

  • Start by calculating your monthly take-home income accurately
  • List fixed expenses first, then estimate variable expenses based on past spending
  • Include savings as a budget line item, not an afterthought
  • Your total expenses plus savings must not exceed your income
  • Adjust budget amounts until everything balances
  • Write your budget down and track spending against it throughout the month
  • Review and adjust monthly based on actual results
  • Start simple and be realistic rather than overly optimistic
