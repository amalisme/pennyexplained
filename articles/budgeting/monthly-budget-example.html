Monthly Budget Example

7 min read Budgeting

This article shows three complete monthly budget examples with realistic numbers. Each example represents a different income level and life situation, giving you a practical template to adapt for your own finances.

Seeing real budget numbers helps you understand how budgets work in practice and gives you ideas for organizing your own money.

Example 1: Single Person, $3,200 Monthly Income

Alex is 26, works in retail management, and lives in a mid-sized city. Monthly take-home pay is $3,200 after taxes.

Alex's Monthly Budget

MONTHLY INCOME: $3,200

HOUSING ($1,260)

  • Rent: $1,100
  • Renters insurance: $20
  • Electric: $85
  • Water/trash: $35
  • Internet: $60

TRANSPORTATION ($405)

  • Car payment: $280
  • Car insurance: $125
  • Gas: $100
  • Parking: $0

FOOD ($520)

  • Groceries: $350
  • Eating out: $170

PERSONAL ($210)

  • Phone: $75
  • Haircuts: $40
  • Toiletries/personal care: $50
  • Clothing: $45

ENTERTAINMENT & LIFESTYLE ($155)

  • Streaming services: $35
  • Gym membership: $45
  • Entertainment/hobbies: $75

DEBT & SAVINGS ($450)

  • Student loan payment: $150
  • Emergency fund: $200
  • Vacation savings: $100

MISCELLANEOUS ($100)

  • Unexpected expenses buffer: $100

TOTAL EXPENSES: $3,100

REMAINING: $100

(Goes to emergency fund at month end)

What This Budget Shows

Alex's budget prioritizes building savings while still allowing for some fun. Housing takes up about 34% of income, which is high but typical for many renters.

The $100 miscellaneous category acts as a buffer for things like car maintenance, medical copays, or other items that don't fit neatly elsewhere.

Example 2: Couple, $5,800 Monthly Income

Jordan and Casey are married with no kids. Jordan earns $3,400 monthly, Casey earns $2,400, for a combined household income of $5,800.

Jordan & Casey's Monthly Budget

MONTHLY INCOME: $5,800

HOUSING ($1,830)

  • Rent: $1,500
  • Renters insurance: $30
  • Electric: $120
  • Water/trash: $40
  • Internet: $70
  • Natural gas: $70

TRANSPORTATION ($705)

  • Car payment 1: $320
  • Car payment 2: $0 (paid off)
  • Car insurance (both cars): $230
  • Gas: $155

FOOD ($850)

  • Groceries: $600
  • Eating out/date nights: $250

PERSONAL ($340)

  • Phone (2 lines): $120
  • Haircuts (both): $70
  • Toiletries/personal care: $80
  • Clothing: $70

ENTERTAINMENT & LIFESTYLE ($265)

  • Streaming services: $45
  • Gym memberships (both): $80
  • Entertainment/hobbies: $140

HEALTH ($220)

  • Prescription medications: $60
  • Medical copays/expenses: $80
  • Dental/vision: $80

DEBT & SAVINGS ($1,350)

  • Student loans: $280
  • Emergency fund: $500
  • Retirement (IRA): $400
  • House down payment: $170

MISCELLANEOUS ($240)

  • Gifts/holidays: $100
  • Pet expenses: $60
  • Unexpected expenses: $80

TOTAL EXPENSES: $5,800

REMAINING: $0

What This Budget Shows

This couple budgets every dollar with a zero-based approach. They prioritize savings heavily at 23% of income, working toward multiple goals simultaneously.

Having one paid-off car reduces their transportation costs significantly. They also budget for irregular expenses like gifts throughout the year rather than scrambling during holidays.

Example 3: Single Parent, $4,200 Monthly Income

Taylor is a single parent with one child (age 7), working as a teacher. Monthly take-home income is $4,200.

Taylor's Monthly Budget

MONTHLY INCOME: $4,200

HOUSING ($1,495)

  • Rent (2-bedroom): $1,250
  • Renters insurance: $25
  • Electric: $95
  • Water/trash: $45
  • Internet: $80

TRANSPORTATION ($465)

  • Car payment: $305
  • Car insurance: $160
  • Gas: $100

CHILDCARE & EDUCATION ($520)

  • After-school care: $400
  • School supplies/fees: $50
  • Child activities: $70

FOOD ($600)

  • Groceries: $500
  • Eating out (occasional): $100

PERSONAL ($235)

  • Phone: $85
  • Haircuts (both): $50
  • Toiletries/personal care: $60
  • Clothing (both): $40

CHILD-SPECIFIC ($180)

  • Child clothing: $50
  • Toys/books: $40
  • Child medical/dental: $90

ENTERTAINMENT ($100)

  • Streaming services: $30
  • Family activities: $70

DEBT & SAVINGS ($500)

  • Credit card payment: $150
  • Emergency fund: $250
  • Child's savings: $100

MISCELLANEOUS ($105)

  • Gifts/birthdays: $50
  • Unexpected expenses: $55

TOTAL EXPENSES: $4,200

REMAINING: $0

What This Budget Shows

Single parent budgets often include significant childcare costs. Taylor's after-school care alone is $400 monthly, nearly 10% of income.

Despite tight finances, Taylor still budgets for savings and pays extra on credit card debt. The entertainment budget is modest but ensures quality time with the child.

Comparing the Three Examples

Looking at these budgets side by side reveals common patterns and differences:

Housing Percentages

  • Alex: 34% of income
  • Jordan & Casey: 26% of income
  • Taylor: 30% of income

Combined income helps Jordan and Casey keep housing lower percentage-wise, even though they pay the most in absolute dollars.

Savings Rates

  • Alex: 9% ($300)
  • Jordan & Casey: 18% ($1,070)
  • Taylor: 8% ($350)

Higher income allows Jordan and Casey to save more. Taylor saves less percentage-wise but still prioritizes it despite childcare costs.

Food Spending

  • Alex: $520 (16% of income)
  • Jordan & Casey: $850 (15% of income)
  • Taylor: $600 (14% of income)

Food costs scale with household size but also represent a category where personal choices vary widely.

How to Adapt These Examples

Use these examples as templates, not exact models to copy. Your budget will differ based on:

  • Where you live (housing and cost of living vary greatly)
  • Your income level
  • Debt obligations
  • Personal priorities and goals
  • Family size and situation

Making It Your Own

Pick the example closest to your situation. Replace their numbers with your actual costs. Add or remove categories based on your needs.

If you have a pet, add a pet category. If you don't have a car, remove transportation. If you pay for health insurance separately, add that line.

Common Budget Categories

Most budgets include variations of these main categories:

  • Housing: Rent/mortgage, utilities, insurance
  • Transportation: Car payment, insurance, gas, maintenance
  • Food: Groceries and eating out
  • Personal: Phone, clothing, personal care
  • Entertainment: Subscriptions, hobbies, activities
  • Savings: Emergency fund, goals, retirement
  • Debt: Credit cards, loans, student debt
  • Miscellaneous: Buffer for irregular expenses

What These Budgets Don't Show

These are simplified examples. Real budgets often include additional complexity:

  • Irregular annual expenses (car registration, property tax)
  • Seasonal variation (higher utilities in summer/winter)
  • Irregular income (freelance, commission)
  • Major one-time expenses (car repair, medical procedure)

Experienced budgeters set aside money monthly for these irregular expenses so they're ready when they occur.

Key Takeaways

  • Budgets vary widely based on income, location, and personal situation
  • Housing typically takes 25-35% of income for most people
  • All three examples prioritize some level of savings despite different income levels
  • Buffer categories for miscellaneous expenses prevent budget failure
  • Combined household income can reduce percentage spent on fixed costs
  • Children add significant expenses, especially childcare
  • Use these examples as templates but customize for your actual situation
  • Every dollar should have an assignment in a complete budget
